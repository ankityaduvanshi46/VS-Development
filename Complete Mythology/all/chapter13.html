Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 13: Kshetra Kshetrajna Vibhaga Yoga

Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 13: Kshetra Kshetrajna Vibhaga Yoga

The Yoga of the Distinction Between the Field and the Knower of the Field

Chapter Insights

Introduction

Chapter 13 of the Bhagavad Gita, known as Kshetra Kshetrajna Vibhaga Yoga, explores the distinction between the material body (the field) and the soul (the knower of the field). Lord Krishna explains the nature of the physical world, the self, and the means to liberation through knowledge (Jnana Yoga). By understanding the difference between the transient body and the eternal soul, and cultivating virtues like humility and detachment, one can attain freedom from material bondage. This chapter provides profound philosophical insights for spiritual seekers.

Chapter 13’s focus on self-knowledge and detachment remains highly relevant for navigating the complexities of modern life with clarity and purpose.

Historical and Philosophical Context

Embedded in the Mahabharata, Chapter 13 draws heavily from Upanishadic teachings, particularly the concepts of Prakriti (matter) and Purusha (consciousness), aligning with Sankhya philosophy. Krishna’s discourse (13.2-6) integrates Jnana Yoga with spiritual wisdom, addressing Arjuna’s need for clarity on the nature of existence amidst his warrior duties on the Kurukshetra battlefield.

Key Themes

  • Kshetra and Kshetrajna: The body is the field, and the soul is its knower, distinct and eternal.
  • Nature of Prakriti: The material world, including senses and elements, is transient and subject to change.
  • Knowledge as Liberation: True knowledge of the self and matter leads to freedom from bondage.
  • Virtues of a Knower: Humility, detachment, and equanimity are essential for spiritual growth.
  • Role of the Supreme: Krishna as the ultimate knower pervades all fields, uniting matter and spirit.
  • Practical Wisdom: Discriminative knowledge enables one to transcend material limitations.

Psychological Insights

Chapter 13’s distinction between body and soul (13.2) aligns with psychological practices of self-awareness, reducing identification with transient emotions. The virtues listed (13.8-12), such as humility and non-attachment, promote emotional resilience and mindfulness, fostering mental clarity. Krishna’s emphasis on eternal consciousness (13.13) provides existential stability, alleviating fears of impermanence.

Cross-Cultural Relevance

The chapter’s focus on the eternal soul resonates with concepts of the self in Buddhism and Jainism, while its emphasis on knowledge parallels Platonic and Stoic ideas of understanding reality. The universal pursuit of self-realization (13.24) aligns with mystical traditions across cultures, making Chapter 13 a bridge for interfaith exploration.

Philosophical Debates

Chapter 13 sparks discussions on the duality of matter and consciousness (13.2-6) versus non-dual Advaita perspectives. The role of knowledge versus devotion in liberation (13.8-12) fuels debates on spiritual paths, enriching metaphysical discourse on the nature of reality.

Relevance Today

In 2025, Chapter 13’s teachings guide navigating a materialistic world by fostering detachment and self-awareness. Its emphasis on virtues like humility and equanimity promotes ethical living and compassion, addressing modern challenges like stress, consumerism, and environmental crises.

Commentators’ Perspectives

Adi Shankaracharya (8th Century)

Shankaracharya views Chapter 13 as a guide to non-dual realization, interpreting the distinction between Kshetra and Kshetrajna (13.2) as a means to understand the unity of the self with Brahman.

Ramanujacharya (11th Century)

Ramanujacharya sees Chapter 13 as emphasizing the soul’s dependence on Krishna, with knowledge of Kshetrajna (13.2) leading to devotion in Vishishtadvaita.

Mahatma Gandhi (20th Century)

Gandhi applied Chapter 13’s teachings on detachment and humility (13.8-12) to his life, using self-knowledge to guide his non-violent resistance and service.

Ethical Dilemmas and Resolutions

Arjuna’s Conflict

Arjuna struggles with his identity and duties as a warrior. Krishna resolves this by teaching the distinction between the transient body and eternal soul (13.2), enabling Arjuna to act without attachment to outcomes.

Modern Applications

Chapter 13 applies to dilemmas like materialism versus spirituality. For example, practicing detachment (13.9) in professional life can lead to ethical decisions and inner peace.

Meditation and Practice

Jnana Yoga Practice

Reflect on the distinction between the body and soul (13.2) through self-inquiry, cultivating awareness of the eternal self to transcend material identification.

Bhakti Yoga Practice

Meditate on Krishna as the supreme knower (13.3), offering actions to him to foster devotion and detachment from material outcomes.

Cultural Impact

Literature and Arts

Chapter 13 has inspired Indian philosophical texts and art, with themes of the soul and matter depicted in Upanishadic commentaries and temple teachings.

Global Influence

The chapter’s focus on self-knowledge has influenced global spiritual movements, including Vedanta societies, and shaped interfaith dialogues on consciousness and reality.

Shlokas

Below is a selection of 20 key shlokas from Chapter 13, presented in Sanskrit with Hindi and English translations, accompanied by commentaries to highlight their significance.

Shloka 13.1

अर्जुन उवाच
प्रकृतिं पुरुषं चैव क्षेत्रं क्षेत्रज्ञमेव च।
एतद्वेदितुमिच्छामि ज्ञानं ज्ञेयं च केशव।।

अर्जुन बोले: हे केशव! मैं प्रकृति, पुरुष, क्षेत्र, क्षेत्रज्ञ, ज्ञान और ज्ञेय को जानना चाहता हूँ।

Arjuna said: O Keshava, I wish to know about Prakriti, Purusha, the field, the knower of the field, knowledge, and the object of knowledge.

Commentary: Arjuna’s inquiry sets the stage for Krishna’s discourse on the distinction between matter and consciousness.

Shloka 13.2

श्री भगवानुवाच
इदं शरीरं कौन्तेय क्षेत्रमित्यभिधीयते।
एतद्यो वेत्ति तं प्राहुः क्षेत्रज्ञ इति तद्विदः।।

श्री भगवान बोले: हे कुन्तीपुत्र! यह शरीर क्षेत्र कहलाता है, और जो इसे जानता है, उसे तत्त्वज्ञ लोग क्षेत्रज्ञ कहते हैं।

The Blessed Lord said: O son of Kunti, this body is called the field, and one who knows it is called the knower of the field by those who know.

Commentary: Krishna introduces the key distinction between the body (matter) and the soul (consciousness).

Shloka 13.3

क्षेत्रज्ञं चापि मां विद्धि सर्वक्षेत्रेषु भारत।
क्षेत्रक्षेत्रज्ञयोर्ज्ञानं यत्तज्ज्ञानं मतं मम।।

हे भारत! सभी क्षेत्रों में मुझे क्षेत्रज्ञ जान। क्षेत्र और क्षेत्रज्ञ का ज्ञान ही मेरे मत में ज्ञान है।

O Bharata, know Me as the knower of the field in all fields. The knowledge of the field and its knower is true knowledge, in My opinion.

Commentary: Krishna identifies himself as the supreme knower, emphasizing that true knowledge discerns matter and spirit.

Shloka 13.4

तत्क्षेत्रं यच्च यादृक् च यद्विकारी यतश्च यत्।
स च यो यत्प्रभावश्च तत्समासेन मे शृणु।।

वह क्षेत्र क्या है, कैसा है, उसके विकार क्या हैं, वह कहाँ से उत्पन्न हुआ, और वह क्षेत्रज्ञ कौन है, उसका प्रभाव क्या है, यह संक्षेप में मुझसे सुन।

Hear from Me briefly what the field is, its nature, modifications, source, and who the knower is, and what his powers are.

Commentary: Krishna promises a concise explanation of the field and its knower, guiding Arjuna toward self-knowledge.

Shloka 13.5

ऋषिभिर्बहुधा गीतं छन्दोभिर्विविधैः पृथक्।
ब्रह्मसूत्रपदैश्चैव हेतुमद्भिर्विनिश्चितैः।।

ऋषियों ने इसे अनेक प्रकार से गाया, विभिन्न छंदों में और ब्रह्मसूत्र के तर्कपूर्ण पदों में निश्चित रूप से वर्णित किया।

Sages have sung of it in many ways, in various hymns and in the definitive words of the Brahma Sutras with logical reasoning.

Commentary: Krishna validates his teachings with ancient wisdom, affirming their philosophical authority.

Shloka 13.6

महाभूतान्यहङ्कारो बुद्धिरव्यक्तमेव च।
इन्द्रियाणि दशैकं च पञ्च चेन्द्रियगोचराः।।

महाभूत, अहंकार, बुद्धि, अव्यक्त, दस इन्द्रियाँ, एक मन, और पाँच इन्द्रिय विषय—ये क्षेत्र हैं।

The great elements, ego, intellect, the unmanifest, the ten senses, the mind, and the five objects of the senses.

Commentary: Krishna begins describing the components of the field, outlining the material aspects of existence.

Shloka 13.7

इच्छा द्वेषः सुखं दुःखं सङ्घातश्चेतना धृतिः।
एतत्क्षेत्रं समासेन सविकारमुदाहृतम्।।

इच्छा, द्वेष, सुख, दुख, समूह, चेतना और धृति—यह क्षेत्र अपने विकारों सहित संक्षेप में कहा गया है।

Desire, aversion, pleasure, pain, the aggregate, consciousness, and fortitude—this field with its modifications is briefly described.

Commentary: Krishna completes the description of the field, including its psychological and physical aspects.

Shloka 13.8

अमानित्वमदम्भित्वमहिंसा क्षान्तिरार्जवम्।
आचार्योपासनं शौचं स्थैर्यमात्मविनिग्रहः।।

नम्रता, दिखावा न करना, अहिंसा, क्षमा, सरलता, आचार्य की सेवा, शुद्धता, स्थिरता और आत्मसंयम।

Humility, sincerity, non-violence, patience, uprightness, service to the teacher, purity, steadfastness, and self-control.

Commentary: Krishna begins listing qualities of true knowledge, emphasizing virtues that lead to liberation.

Shloka 13.9

इन्द्रियार्थेषु वैराग्यमनहङ्कार एव च।
जन्ममृत्युजराव्याधिदुःखदोषानुदर्शनम्।।

इन्द्रिय विषयों में वैराग्य, अहंकार का अभाव, और जन्म, मृत्यु, जरा, व्याधि और दुख के दोषों का अनुशीलन।

Detachment from sense objects, absence of ego, and reflection on the miseries of birth, death, old age, disease, and pain.

Commentary: Krishna continues defining knowledge, highlighting detachment and awareness of life’s impermanence.

Shloka 13.10

असक्तिरनभिष्वङ्गः पुत्रदारगृहादिषु।
नित्यं च समचित्तत्वमिष्टानिष्टोपपत्तिषु।।

पुत्र, पत्नी, गृह आदि में आसक्ति का अभाव, और इष्ट-अनिष्ट प्राप्तियों में सदा समचित्तता।

Non-attachment, lack of identification with son, wife, home, and the like, and constant even-mindedness in desired and undesired events.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes non-attachment and equanimity as key to spiritual knowledge.

Shloka 13.11

मयि चानन्ययोगेन भक्तिरव्यभिचारिणी।
विविक्तदेशसेवित्वमरतिर्जनसंसदि।।

मुझमें अनन्य योग से अव्यभिचारी भक्ति, एकान्त स्थान में रुचि, और जनसमूह में अरुचि।

Unswerving devotion to Me with single-minded Yoga, preference for solitude, and disinterest in worldly gatherings.

Commentary: Krishna includes devotion and solitude as components of knowledge, bridging Jnana and Bhakti.

Shloka 13.12

अध्यात्मज्ञाननित्यत्वं तत्त्वज्ञानार्थदर्शनम्।
एतज्ज्ञानमिति प्रोक्तमज्ञानं यदतोऽन्यथा।।

अध्यात्म ज्ञान में नित्यता और तत्त्वज्ञान के उद्देश्य का दर्शन—यह ज्ञान कहा गया है, और इसके विपरीत अज्ञान है।

Constancy in self-knowledge and insight into the goal of true knowledge—this is declared as knowledge; anything else is ignorance.

Commentary: Krishna concludes the definition of knowledge, contrasting it with ignorance to guide spiritual practice.

Shloka 13.13

ज्ञेयं यत्तत्प्रवक्ष्यामि यज्ज्ञात्वामृतमश्नुते।
अनादिमत्परं ब्रह्म न सत्तन्नासदुच्यते।।

मैं उस ज्ञेय का वर्णन करूँगा, जिसे जानकर अमृत प्राप्त होता है। वह अनादि परम ब्रह्म है, जो न सत् है न असत् कहलाता है।

I will declare that which is to be known, knowing which one attains immortality—the beginningless supreme Brahman, neither being nor non-being.

Commentary: Krishna introduces the supreme reality, emphasizing its transcendental nature and liberating knowledge.

Shloka 13.14

सर्वतः पाणिपादं तत्सर्वतोऽक्षिशिरोमुखम्।
सर्वतः श्रुतिमल्लोके सर्वमावृत्य तिष्ठति।।

वह सर्वत्र हाथ-पैर वाला, सर्वत्र नेत्र, सिर और मुख वाला, सर्वत्र श्रवण वाला है, और विश्व में सब कुछ आवृत करके स्थित है।

With hands and feet everywhere, with eyes, heads, and mouths everywhere, with ears everywhere, it exists, pervading all in the world.

Commentary: Krishna describes the all-pervading nature of Brahman, inspiring awe and contemplation.

Shloka 13.15

सर्वेन्द्रियगुणाभासं सर्वेन्द्रियविवर्जितम्।
असक्तं सर्वभृच्चैव निर्गुणं गुणभोक्तृ च।।

वह सभी इन्द्रियों के गुणों का आभास देने वाला, फिर भी इन्द्रियों से रहित, असक्त, सभी का पोषक, निर्गुण और गुणों का भोक्ता है।

Shining by the functions of all senses, yet without senses; unattached, yet supporting all; devoid of qualities, yet their enjoyer.

Commentary: Krishna highlights Brahman’s paradoxical nature, transcending yet supporting creation.

Shloka 13.16

बाहिरन्तश्च भूतानामचरं चरमेव च।
सूक्ष्मत्वात्तदविज्ञेयं दूरस्थं चान्तिके च तत्।।

वह प्राणियों के बाहर और भीतर, चर और अचर, सूक्ष्म होने से अविज्ञेय, दूर और निकट में भी स्थित है।

Outside and within all beings, moving and unmoving, incomprehensible due to its subtlety, far and near—it is that.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes Brahman’s omnipresence, encouraging contemplation of its subtle nature.

Shloka 13.17

अविभक्तं च भूतेषु विभक्तमिव च स्थितम्।
भूतभर्तृ च तज्ज्ञेयं ग्रसिष्णु प्रभविष्णु च।।

वह प्राणियों में अविभक्त और विभक्त-सा स्थित, प्राणियों का पोषक, ग्रास करने वाला और उत्पन्न करने वाला है, उसे ज्ञेय जान।

Undivided yet appearing as divided in beings, supporting them, devouring and generating—it is to be known.

Commentary: Krishna describes Brahman’s unity and role in creation, dissolution, and sustenance.

Shloka 13.18

ज्योतिषामपि तज्ज्योतिस्तमसः परमुच्यते।
ज्ञानं ज्ञेयं ज्ञानगम्यं हृदि सर्वस्य विष्ठितम्।।

वह ज्योतियों का भी ज्योति, तम से परे कहा जाता है। वह ज्ञान, ज्ञेय और ज्ञान से प्राप्त होने वाला है, जो सभी के हृदय में स्थित है।

The light of all lights, said to be beyond darkness; it is knowledge, the knowable, and attainable by knowledge, seated in the hearts of all.

Commentary: Krishna portrays Brahman as the ultimate light of knowledge, residing in all beings.

Shloka 13.24

यो मां एवमसम्मूढो जानाति पुरुषोत्तमम्।
स सर्वविद्भजति मां सर्वभावेन भारत।।

जो असम्मूढ होकर मुझे पुरुषोत्तम जानता है, वह सर्वज्ञ होकर सभी भावों से मुझे भजता है, हे भारत।

One who, undeluded, knows Me as the Supreme Person, knows all and worships Me with all their being, O Bharata.

Commentary: Krishna links knowledge of his supreme nature to devotion, uniting Jnana and Bhakti.

Shloka 13.35

क्षेत्रक्षेत्रज्ञयोर्ज्ञानं यत्तज्ज्ञानं मतं मम।
यद्विज्ञानेन परं विश्वं विश्वेन संनादति।।

क्षेत्र और क्षेत्रज्ञ के ज्ञान को जो जानता है, वह मेरे मत में ज्ञान है। जिस विज्ञान से परम विश्व विश्व के साथ संनादति है।

The knowledge of the field and its knower is true knowledge, in My opinion. Through that knowledge, the supreme universe resonates with the universe.

Commentary: Krishna concludes that understanding the field and knower leads to ultimate wisdom and liberation.
