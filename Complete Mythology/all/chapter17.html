Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 17: Shraddha Traya Vibhaga Yoga

Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 17: Shraddha Traya Vibhaga Yoga

The Yoga of the Division of Threefold Faith

Chapter Insights

Introduction

Chapter 17 of the Bhagavad Gita, known as Shraddha Traya Vibhaga Yoga or the Yoga of the Division of Threefold Faith, explores the influence of the three gunas (sattva, rajas, and tamas) on faith, worship, food, sacrifice, austerity, and charity. Lord Krishna responds to Arjuna’s question about the nature of faith performed without scriptural guidance, explaining how the gunas shape human actions and spiritual practices. This chapter provides practical guidance for aligning actions with sattvic principles to attain spiritual growth.

In 2025, Chapter 17 offers insights for cultivating mindful living, ethical choices, and spiritual discipline in a world driven by materialism and instant gratification.

Historical and Philosophical Context

Rooted in the Mahabharata, Chapter 17 builds on the Samkhya philosophy of the three gunas introduced in Chapter 14. Krishna elaborates on their practical application in daily life and spiritual practices (17.2-4), addressing Arjuna’s doubts about authentic worship during the Kurukshetra war. The chapter integrates Vedic rituals with the path of devotion and self-discipline.

Key Themes

  • Threefold Faith: Faith is shaped by sattva, rajas, or tamas, influencing spiritual and worldly actions (17.2).
  • Sattvic Practices: Actions rooted in purity, such as sattvic food, sacrifice, and charity, lead to spiritual progress (17.7-20).
  • Role of Intention: The quality of intention behind actions determines their spiritual value (17.11-13).
  • Discipline and Austerity: Austerity of body, speech, and mind, when sattvic, purifies the soul (17.14-16).
  • Om Tat Sat: These sacred syllables represent the divine, sanctifying actions performed with faith (17.23-28).

Psychological Insights

The chapter’s focus on faith and intention aligns with psychological studies on intrinsic motivation and mindfulness. Sattvic faith fosters clarity and purpose, while rajasic and tamasic tendencies lead to restlessness or delusion (17.2-4). Applying these insights helps individuals cultivate positive habits and emotional resilience in modern challenges like stress or ethical dilemmas.

Cross-Cultural Relevance

The threefold classification of faith resonates with ethical frameworks in Christianity, Buddhism, and other traditions, where intention shapes moral actions. The emphasis on sattvic discipline (17.14-16) parallels mindfulness practices globally, making Chapter 17 a universal guide for intentional living.

Philosophical Debates

Chapter 17 raises questions about the balance between scriptural adherence and personal faith (17.1). Krishna’s emphasis on intention (17.11-13) sparks discussions on whether actions without scriptural basis can be spiritually valid, enriching debates on ethics and spirituality.

Relevance Today

In 2025, Chapter 17’s teachings guide ethical decision-making in a polarized world. The focus on sattvic practices encourages sustainable living, mindful consumption, and compassionate charity, fostering harmony in personal and societal contexts.

Commentators’ Perspectives

Adi Shankaracharya (8th Century)

Shankaracharya interprets sattvic faith as a step toward self-realization, emphasizing knowledge over ritualistic actions (17.2).

Ramanujacharya (11th Century)

Ramanujacharya views sattvic practices as expressions of devotion to Krishna, leading to divine grace and liberation (17.26).

Mahatma Gandhi (20th Century)

Gandhi applied Chapter 17’s teachings to practice sattvic austerity and charity, aligning his actions with truth and non-violence.

Ethical Dilemmas and Resolutions

Arjuna’s Conflict

Arjuna questions the validity of faith without scriptural guidance (17.1). Krishna resolves this by emphasizing intention and sattvic faith, guiding Arjuna to act with purity and devotion (17.11).

Modern Applications

Chapter 17 applies to dilemmas like ethical consumption. Choosing sattvic practices, such as sustainable diets or selfless charity, aligns actions with spiritual and societal good, balancing personal desires with collective well-being.

Meditation and Practice

Bhakti Yoga Practice

Chant “Om Tat Sat” (17.23) during worship or charity to sanctify actions, cultivating sattvic faith.

Jnana Yoga Practice

Reflect on the gunas’ influence on daily actions (17.7-10), practicing mindfulness to foster sattvic habits and detach from rajas and tamas.

Cultural Impact

Literature and Arts

Chapter 17’s teachings on sattvic practices have influenced Indian literature, yoga, and dietary traditions, shaping cultural emphasis on purity and discipline.

Global Influence

The chapter’s focus on intention and mindful action has inspired global wellness movements, promoting ethical living and mindfulness across cultures.

Shlokas

Below is a selection of 20 key shlokas from Chapter 17, presented in Sanskrit with Hindi and English translations, accompanied by commentaries to highlight their significance.

Shloka 17.1

अर्जुन उवाच
ये शास्त्रविधिमुत्सृज्य यजन्ते श्रद्धयान्विताः।
तेषां निष्ठा तु का कृष्ण सत्त्वमाहो रजस्तमः।।

अर्जुन बोले: हे कृष्ण! जो लोग शास्त्रविधि को छोड़कर श्रद्धा से यजन करते हैं, उनकी निष्ठा क्या है—सत्त्व, रजस् या तमस्?

Arjuna said: O Krishna, what is the state of those who worship with faith but disregard scriptural injunctions? Is it sattva, rajas, or tamas?

Commentary: Arjuna’s question sets the stage for Krishna’s discourse on the nature of faith influenced by the gunas.

Shloka 17.2

श्री भगवानुवाच
त्रिविधा भवति श्रद्धा देहिनां सा स्वभावजा।
सात्त्विकी राजसी चैव तामसी चेति तां शृणु।।

श्री भगवान बोले: देहधारियों की श्रद्धा स्वभाव से तीन प्रकार की होती है—सात्त्विकी, राजसी और तामसी। इसे सुन।

The Blessed Lord said: The faith of embodied beings is of three kinds—sattvic, rajasic, and tamasic, born of their nature. Listen to this.

Commentary: Krishna introduces the threefold nature of faith, shaped by the gunas, setting the framework for the chapter.

Shloka 17.3

सत्त्वानुरूपा सर्वस्य श्रद्धा भवति भारत।
श्रद्धामयोऽयं पुरुषो यो यच्छ्रद्धः स एव सः।।

हे भारत! सभी की श्रद्धा उनके स्वभाव के अनुरूप होती है। यह पुरुष श्रद्धामय है; जैसी उसकी श्रद्धा, वैसा ही वह है।

O Bharata, the faith of all is according to their nature. A person is made of faith; as is their faith, so are they.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes that faith defines a person’s character, shaped by their inherent guna.

Shloka 17.4

यजन्ते सात्त्विका देवान्यक्षरक्षांसि राजसाः।
प्रेतान्भूतगणांश्चान्ये यजन्ते तामसा जनाः।।

सात्त्विक लोग देवताओं की पूजा करते हैं, राजसिक लोग यक्षों और राक्षसों की, और तामसिक लोग प्रेतों और भूतगणों की पूजा करते हैं।

Sattvic people worship the gods, rajasic people worship yakshas and rakshasas, and tamasic people worship ghosts and spirits.

Commentary: The objects of worship reflect the guna influencing one’s faith, guiding spiritual practice.

Shloka 17.5

अशास्त्रविहितं घोरं तप्यन्ते ये तपो जनाः।
दम्भाहङ्कारसंयुक्ताः कामरागबलान्विताः।।

जो लोग शास्त्रविरुद्ध भयंकर तप करते हैं, दम्भ और अहंकार से युक्त, काम और राग के बल से प्रेरित हैं,।

Those who perform severe austerities not enjoined by scripture, driven by hypocrisy and egoism, impelled by desire and passion,.

Commentary: Krishna warns against misguided austerities driven by rajas and tamas, lacking spiritual authenticity.

Shloka 17.6

कर्षयन्तः शरीरस्थं भूतग्राममचेतसः।
मां चैवान्तःशरीरस्थं तान्विद्ध्यासुरनिश्चयान्।।

जो अज्ञानी लोग शरीर में स्थित भूतसमूह को और मुझ अन्तर्यामी को कष्ट देते हैं, उन्हें आसुरी स्वभाव वाला जान।

Those senseless ones who torment the elements in the body and Me dwelling within, know them to have demonic resolves.

Commentary: Harmful practices are condemned as demonic, emphasizing respect for the body and soul.

Shloka 17.7

आहारस्त्वपि सर्वस्य त्रिविधो भवति प्रियः।
यज्ञस्तपस्तथा दानं तेषां भेदमिमं शृणु।।

सभी का आहार भी तीन प्रकार का प्रिय होता है, और यज्ञ, तप और दान भी। उनके भेद को सुन।

The food dear to all is also of three kinds, as are sacrifice, austerity, and charity. Listen to their distinctions.

Commentary: Krishna introduces the threefold nature of daily practices, linking them to the gunas.

Shloka 17.8

आयुःसत्त्वबलारोग्यसुखप्रीतिविवर्धनाः।
रस्याः स्निग्धाः स्थिरा हृद्या आहाराः सात्त्विकप्रियाः।।

आयु, सत्त्व, बल, आरोग्य, सुख और प्रीति बढ़ाने वाले, रसयुक्त, स्निग्ध, स्थिर और हृदय को प्रिय आहार सात्त्विक लोगों को प्रिय हैं।

Foods that promote longevity, vitality, strength, health, happiness, and satisfaction, juicy, wholesome, and pleasing, are dear to the sattvic.

Commentary: Sattvic food is described as nourishing and balanced, promoting physical and spiritual well-being.

Shloka 17.9

कट्वम्ललवणात्युष्णतीक्ष्णरूक्षविदाहिनः।
आहारा राजसस्येष्टा दुःखशोकामयप्रदाः।।

कड़वे, खट्टे, लवणयुक्त, अति गर्म, तीक्ष्ण, रूखे और जलन पैदा करने वाले आहार राजसिक लोगों को प्रिय हैं, जो दुख, शोक और रोग देते हैं।

Foods that are bitter, sour, salty, overly hot, pungent, dry, and burning, causing pain, grief, and disease, are dear to the rajasic.

Commentary: Rajasic foods are stimulating but harmful, reflecting the restlessness of rajas.

Shloka 17.10

यातयामं गतरसं पूति पर्युषितं च यत्।
उच्छिष्टमपि चामेध्यं भोजनं तामसप्रियम्।।

बासी, स्वादहीन, दुर्गंधयुक्त, सड़ा हुआ, और उच्छिष्ट तथा अपवित्र भोजन तामसिक लोगों को प्रिय है।

Food that is stale, tasteless, putrid, spoiled, leftover, or impure is dear to the tamasic.

Commentary: Tamasic food is described as detrimental, reflecting the ignorance of tamas.

Shloka 17.11

अफलाकाङ्क्षिभिर्यज्ञो विधिदृष्टो य इज्यते।
यष्टव्यमेवेति मनः समाधाय स सात्त्विकः।।

जो यज्ञ फल की इच्छा के बिना, शास्त्रविधि से किया जाता है, केवल यजन करना चाहिए इस मन को समाधान कर, वह सात्त्विक है।

The sacrifice performed according to scriptural injunctions, without desire for reward, with the mind focused on it as a duty, is sattvic.

Commentary: Sattvic sacrifice is selfless and scripturally aligned, emphasizing pure intention.

Shloka 17.12

अभिसन्धाय तु फलं दम्भार्थमपि चैव यत्।
इज्यते भरतश्रेष्ठ तं यज्ञं विद्धि राजसम्।।

हे भरतश्रेष्ठ! जो यज्ञ फल की कामना और दम्भ के लिए किया जाता है, उसे राजसिक जान।

O best of Bharatas, the sacrifice performed for some reward or out of hypocrisy, know that to be rajasic.

Commentary: Rajasic sacrifice is motivated by ego and desire, lacking spiritual depth.

Shloka 17.13

विधिहीनमसृष्टान्नं मन्त्रहीनमदक्षिणम्।
श्रद्धाविरहितं यज्ञं तामसं परिचक्षते।।

जो यज्ञ शास्त्रविधि, अन्नदान, मंत्र और दक्षिणा से रहित तथा श्रद्धाहीन है, उसे तामसिक कहते हैं।

The sacrifice performed without regard for scripture, without food distribution, mantras, charity, or faith is said to be tamasic.

Commentary: Tamasic sacrifice lacks faith and discipline, rendering it spiritually ineffective.

Shloka 17.14

देवद्विजगुरुप्राज्ञपूजनं शौचमार्जवम्।
ब्रह्मचर्यमहिंसा च शारीरं तप उच्यते।।

देवता, द्विज, गुरु और प्राज्ञों का पूजन, शौच, आर्जव, ब्रह्मचर्य और अहिंसा—यह शारीरिक तप कहा जाता है।

Worship of gods, brahmins, gurus, and the wise, purity, straightforwardness, celibacy, and non-violence are called bodily austerity.

Commentary: Krishna defines sattvic physical austerity, emphasizing ethical and disciplined living.

Shloka 17.15

अनुद्वेगकरं वाक्यं सत्यं प्रियहितं च यत्।
स्वाध्यायाभ्यसनं चैव वाङ्मयं तप उच्यते।।

जो वचन उद्वेग न करने वाला, सत्य, प्रिय और हितकारी हो, और स्वाध्याय का अभ्यास—यह वाचिक तप कहा जाता है।

Speech that causes no distress, is truthful, pleasant, and beneficial, and the practice of scriptural study is called verbal austerity.

Commentary: Sattvic speech is disciplined and uplifting, fostering harmony and wisdom.

Shloka 17.16

मनःप्रसादः सौम्यत्वं मौनमात्मविनिग्रहः।
भावसंशुद्धिरित्येतत्तपो मानसमुच्यते।।

मन की प्रसन्नता, सौम्यता, मौन, आत्मसंयम और भाव की शुद्धि—यह मानसिक तप कहा जाता है।

Mental serenity, gentleness, silence, self-control, and purity of intention are called mental austerity.

Commentary: Mental austerity involves cultivating a calm and pure mind, essential for spiritual growth.

Shloka 17.17

श्रद्धया परया तप्तं तपस्तत्त्रिविधं नरैः।
अफलाकाङ्क्षिभिर्युक्तैः सात्त्विकं परिचक्षते।।

परम श्रद्धा से युक्त, फल की इच्छा के बिना नरों द्वारा किया गया यह त्रिविध तप सात्त्विक कहलाता है।

This threefold austerity, practiced with supreme faith by those without desire for reward, is called sattvic.

Commentary: Sattvic austerity is selfless and faith-driven, leading to spiritual purification.

Shloka 17.18

सत्कारमानपूजार्थं तपो दम्भेन चैव यत्।
क्रियते तदिह प्रोक्तं राजसं चपलं ध्रुवम्।।

जो तप सम्मान, मान और पूजा के लिए दम्भ से किया जाता है, वह यहाँ राजसिक और चपल कहा जाता है।

Austerity performed for respect, honor, or worship, with hypocrisy, is said to be rajasic, unstable and fleeting.

Commentary: Rajasic austerity is ego-driven and temporary, lacking lasting spiritual value.

Shloka 17.19

मूढग्राहेणात्मनो यत्पीडया क्रियते तपः।
परस्योत्सादनार्थं वा तत्तामसमुदाहृतम्।।

जो तप मूढ़ता से आत्मा को पीड़ा देकर या दूसरों के नाश के लिए किया जाता है, वह तामसिक कहा जाता है।

Austerity performed with foolish stubbornness, causing pain to oneself or harm to others, is said to be tamasic.

Commentary: Tamasic austerity is harmful and misguided, lacking spiritual merit.

Shloka 17.23

ॐ तत्सदिति निर्देशो ब्रह्मणस्त्रिविधः स्मृतः।
ब्राह्मणास्तेन वेदाश्च यज्ञाश्च विहिताः पुरा।।

ॐ तत् सत्—यह ब्रह्म का त्रिविध निर्देश कहा गया है। इसके द्वारा पहले ब्राह्मण, वेद और यज्ञ विहित किए गए।

“Om Tat Sat”—this is said to be the threefold designation of Brahman, by which brahmins, Vedas, and sacrifices were ordained in the past.

Commentary: Krishna introduces the sacred syllables that sanctify spiritual actions, emphasizing their divine significance.

Shloka 17.26

सद्भावे साधुभावे च सदित्येतत्प्रयुज्यते।
प्रशस्ते कर्मणि तथा सच्छब्दः पार्थ युज्यते।।

‘सत्’ शब्द सत्य और साधु भाव में प्रयुक्त होता है, और हे पार्थ! प्रशस्त कर्म में भी ‘सत्’ शब्द का प्रयोग होता है।

The word “Sat” is used in the sense of truth and goodness, and also, O Partha, in praiseworthy actions.

Commentary: “Sat” signifies truth and virtue, sanctifying actions performed with pure intention.
