Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 8: Akshara Brahma Yoga

Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 8: Akshara Brahma Yoga

The Yoga of the Imperishable Brahman

Chapter Insights

Introduction

Chapter 8 of the Bhagavad Gita, known as Akshara Brahma Yoga or the Yoga of the Imperishable Brahman, addresses Arjuna’s questions about Brahman, the self, karma, and the paths to liberation. Lord Krishna explains the eternal nature of the soul, the cycles of creation, and the importance of remembering the divine at the time of death. This chapter emphasizes devotion and mindfulness as means to attain the eternal, guiding devotees toward liberation from the cycle of birth and death.

By clarifying metaphysical concepts and offering practical guidance for spiritual practice, Chapter 8 is a beacon for those seeking to understand life, death, and the divine, relevant for modern spiritual seekers navigating existential questions.

Historical and Philosophical Context

Rooted in the Mahabharata, Chapter 8 draws from Upanishadic teachings on the eternal Brahman and integrates Bhakti Yoga with Jnana Yoga. Krishna’s explanation of the imperishable soul (8.3) and cosmic cycles (8.17) aligns with Vedic cosmology and Advaita Vedanta’s non-dual philosophy. The emphasis on devotion at the moment of death (8.5) resonates with the Bhagavata tradition, making the chapter a bridge between knowledge and devotion in the Kurukshetra war context.

Key Themes

  • Imperishable Brahman: The eternal, unchanging reality is the ultimate goal of spiritual practice.
  • Eternal Soul: The soul (atman) is distinct from the body, transcending death.
  • Cosmic Cycles: Creation and dissolution occur in cycles, governed by divine will.
  • Devotion at Death: Remembering Krishna at the time of death ensures liberation.
  • Two Paths: The path of light (liberation) and the path of darkness (rebirth) depend on one’s spiritual state.
  • Practical Devotion: Constant remembrance of the divine integrates spirituality into daily life.

Psychological Insights

Chapter 8’s focus on mindfulness at death (8.5) aligns with psychological practices for cultivating presence and reducing fear of mortality. The concept of the eternal soul (8.21) fosters resilience by affirming an enduring self beyond physical existence. Krishna’s guidance on transcending cycles (8.16) encourages letting go of attachments, akin to cognitive strategies for emotional freedom in modern psychology.

Cross-Cultural Relevance

The chapter’s teachings on the eternal soul resonate with Christian and Islamic concepts of an immortal soul, while the emphasis on devotion parallels mystical traditions like Sufism. The idea of cosmic cycles (8.17) finds echoes in Buddhist and Jain cosmology, and the focus on mindfulness aligns with global meditation practices, making Chapter 8 universally relevant.

Philosophical Debates

Chapter 8 fuels discussions about the nature of Brahman—personal or impersonal—and the role of devotion versus knowledge in liberation. The distinction between the paths of light and darkness (8.26) raises questions about karma and destiny, enriching debates on free will and divine grace in spiritual philosophy.

Relevance Today

In 2025, Chapter 8’s teachings offer solace in addressing mortality and uncertainty in a fast-changing world. Its emphasis on mindfulness and devotion helps navigate stress from global challenges, while the concept of eternal reality promotes hope and purpose. The chapter’s universal vision fosters unity, encouraging compassionate action in diverse societies.

Commentators’ Perspectives

Adi Shankaracharya (8th Century)

Shankaracharya interprets Chapter 8 as emphasizing the imperishable Brahman, with devotion as a means to realize non-dual reality. He views verses like 8.3 as pointing to the eternal self.

Ramanujacharya (11th Century)

Ramanujacharya sees Chapter 8 as highlighting devotion to Krishna as the Supreme Person, with verses like 8.5 emphasizing surrender for liberation in Vishishtadvaita.

Mahatma Gandhi (20th Century)

Gandhi applied Chapter 8’s teachings on mindfulness and devotion to his life, using remembrance of the divine to maintain equanimity during India’s freedom struggle.

Ethical Dilemmas and Resolutions

Arjuna’s Conflict

Arjuna seeks clarity on metaphysical terms and the path to liberation. Krishna resolves this by defining Brahman, karma, and the soul (8.3-4) and emphasizing devotion at death (8.5), guiding Arjuna to act with divine focus.

Modern Applications

Chapter 8 applies to dilemmas like facing mortality or finding purpose. For instance, practicing mindfulness can help individuals maintain clarity and peace in high-stress situations, aligning actions with spiritual goals.

Meditation and Practice

Bhakti Yoga Practice

Chapter 8’s emphasis on remembering Krishna (8.7) supports practices like chanting or daily prayers to cultivate constant divine awareness.

Jnana Yoga Practice

Reflect on the imperishable Brahman (8.21) through self-inquiry, contemplating the eternal soul to transcend fear of death and material attachment.

Cultural Impact

Literature and Arts

Chapter 8 has inspired Indian art, with themes of eternity and devotion depicted in temple iconography and classical music. Its concepts influence modern spiritual literature and films.

Global Influence

The chapter’s teachings on the eternal soul and devotion have shaped global spiritual movements, influencing yoga practices and interfaith dialogues on immortality and divine connection.

Shlokas

Below is a selection of 20 key shlokas from Chapter 8, presented in Sanskrit with Hindi and English translations, accompanied by commentaries to highlight their significance.

Shloka 8.1

अर्जुन उवाच
किं तद्ब्रह्म किमध्यात्मं किं कर्म पुरुषोत्तम।
अधिभूतं च किं प्रोक्तमधिदैवं किमुच्यते।।

अर्जुन बोले: हे पुरुषोत्तम! वह ब्रह्म क्या है? अध्यात्म क्या है? कर्म क्या है? अधिभूत क्या कहा गया है और अधिदैव क्या कहलाता है?

Arjuna said: O Supreme Person, what is Brahman? What is the self? What is karma? What is Adhibhuta, and what is Adhidaiva?

Commentary: Arjuna’s questions set the stage for Krishna’s metaphysical teachings, addressing core spiritual concepts.

Shloka 8.2

अधियज्ञः कथं कोऽत्र देहेऽस्मिन्मधुसूदन।
प्रयाणकाले च कथं ज्ञेयोऽसि नियतात्मभिः।।

हे मधुसूदन! अधियज्ञ कौन है और इस देह में कैसे है? नियत चित्त वाले लोग मृत्यु के समय तुझे कैसे जानते हैं?

Who is Adhiyajna, and how is He in this body, O Madhusudana? How are You known at the time of death by those of steadfast mind?

Commentary: Arjuna seeks clarity on divine presence and remembrance at death, prompting Krishna’s practical guidance.

Shloka 8.3

श्री भगवानुवाच
अक्षरं ब्रह्म परमं स्वभावोऽध्यात्ममुच्यते।
भूतभावोद्भवकरो विसर्गः कर्मसंज्ञितः।।

श्री भगवान बोले: अक्षर परम ब्रह्म है, स्वभाव को अध्यात्म कहा जाता है, और प्राणियों की उत्पत्ति करने वाला विसर्ग कर्म कहलाता है।

The Blessed Lord said: The imperishable is the Supreme Brahman; its nature is called the self; the offering that causes the origin of beings is called karma.

Commentary: Krishna defines key metaphysical terms, grounding spiritual concepts in eternal reality.

Shloka 8.4

अधिभूतं क्षरो भावः पुरुषश्चाधिदैवतम्।
अधियज्ञोऽहमेवात्र देहे देहभृतां वर।।

अधिभूत क्षर भाव है, पुरुष अधिदैव है, और हे देहधारियों में श्रेष्ठ! मैं ही इस देह में अधियज्ञ हूँ।

Adhibhuta is the perishable nature, Adhidaiva is the cosmic being, and I am Adhiyajna here in the body, O best of the embodied.

Commentary: Krishna clarifies the divine’s presence in creation and the body, fostering a holistic spiritual perspective.

Shloka 8.5

अन्तकाले च मामेव स्मरन्मुक्त्वा कलेवरम्।
यः प्रयाति स मद्भावं याति नास्त्यत्र संशयः।।

जो अंतकाल में मुझे स्मरण करके देह त्यागता है, वह मेरे स्वरूप को प्राप्त करता है, इसमें कोई संदेह नहीं।

One who, at the time of death, remembers Me alone, leaving the body, attains My state; there is no doubt about this.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes the power of divine remembrance at death, offering a practical path to liberation.

Shloka 8.6

यं यं वापि स्मरन्भावं त्यजत्यन्ते कलेवरम्।
तं तमेवैति कौन्तेय सदा तद्भावभावितः।।

हे कुन्तीपुत्र! अंत में जिस-जिस भाव को स्मरण करके देह त्यागता है, वह उसी को प्राप्त करता है, क्योंकि वह सदा उसी भाव में रहा होता है।

O son of Kunti, whatever state one remembers when leaving the body, that state one attains, being always absorbed in that thought.

Commentary: Krishna highlights the influence of thoughts at death, urging mindful cultivation of devotion.

Shloka 8.7

तस्मात्सर्वेषु कालेषु मामनुस्मर युध्य च।
मय्यर्पितमनोबुद्धिर्मामेवैष्यस्यसंशयम्।।

इसलिए हर समय मेरा स्मरण कर और युद्ध कर। मुझमें अर्पित मन और बुद्धि वाला तू निःसंदेह मुझे प्राप्त करेगा।

Therefore, remember Me at all times and fight. With mind and intellect offered to Me, you will undoubtedly attain Me.

Commentary: Krishna integrates devotion with duty, encouraging constant divine awareness in action.

Shloka 8.8

अभ्यासयोगयुक्तेन चेतसा नान्यगामिना।
परमं पुरुषं दिव्यं याति पार्थानुचिन्तयन्।।

हे पार्थ! अभ्यास योग से युक्त, एकाग्र चित्त से मेरा चिन्तन करने वाला परम दिव्य पुरुष को प्राप्त करता है।

O Partha, with the mind engaged in the Yoga of practice, not deviating, one who meditates on Me attains the Supreme Divine Person.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes disciplined meditation, reinforcing devotion as a path to the divine.

Shloka 8.9

कविं पुराणमनुशासितारमणोरणीयांसमनुस्मरेद्यः।
सर्वस्य धातारमचिन्त्यरूपमादित्यवर्णं तमसः परस्तात्।।

जो सर्वज्ञ, प्राचीन, शासक, सूक्ष्म से भी सूक्ष्म, सबके धारक, अचिन्त्य रूप, सूर्य के समान तेजस्वी और अंधकार से परे का स्मरण करता है।

One who remembers the omniscient, ancient, ruler, subtler than the subtle, sustainer of all, of inconceivable form, radiant like the sun, beyond darkness.

Commentary: Krishna describes the divine attributes to focus on, inspiring awe and devotion in meditation.

Shloka 8.10

प्रयाणकाले मनसाचलेन भक्त्या युक्तो योगबलेन चैव।
भ्रुवोर्मध्ये प्राणमावेश्य सम्यक् स तं परं पुरुषं उपैति दिव्यम्।।

मृत्यु के समय अचल मन से, भक्ति और योगबल से युक्त, भ्रूमध्य में प्राण को स्थापित करके, वह परम दिव्य पुरुष को प्राप्त करता है।

At the time of death, with unwavering mind, endowed with devotion and the strength of Yoga, fixing the life-breath between the eyebrows, one attains the Supreme Divine Person.

Commentary: Krishna provides a specific meditation technique for liberation, emphasizing focus and devotion.

Shloka 8.11

यदक्षरं वेदविदो वदन्ति विशन्ति यद्यतयो वीत रागाः।
यदिच्छन्तो ब्रह्मचर्यं चरन्ति तत्ते पदं संग्रहेण प्रवक्ष्ये।।

जो अक्षर वेदज्ञानी कहते हैं, जिसमें संन्यासी प्रवेश करते हैं, और जिसे चाहने वाले ब्रह्मचर्य का पालन करते हैं, उस पद को मैं संक्षेप में बताऊँगा।

That which the knowers of the Vedas call the Imperishable, into which the ascetics free from attachment enter, desiring which they practice celibacy, that state I shall briefly declare to you.

Commentary: Krishna introduces the imperishable state, setting the stage for explaining the path to liberation.

Shloka 8.13

ओमित्येकाक्षरं ब्रह्म व्याहरन्मामनुस्मरन्।
यः प्रयाति त्यजन्देहं स याति परमां गतिम्।।

जो ‘ॐ’ इस एकाक्षर ब्रह्म का उच्चारण करते हुए, मेरा स्मरण करके देह त्यागता है, वह परम गति को प्राप्त करता है।

One who, uttering the single syllable Om, the Brahman, remembering Me, leaves the body, attains the supreme goal.

Commentary: Krishna highlights the power of chanting Om with devotion, a practical practice for liberation.

Shloka 8.14

अनन्यचेताः सततं यो मां स्मरति नित्यशः।
तस्याहं सुलभः पार्थ नित्ययुक्तस्य योगिनः।।

हे पार्थ! जो योगी अनन्य चित्त से सदा मेरा स्मरण करता है, मैं उस नित्य युक्त योगी के लिए सुलभ हूँ।

O Partha, for the yogi who constantly remembers Me with an undistracted mind, I am easily attainable, being ever united with them.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes constant devotion, making liberation accessible through steadfast remembrance.

Shloka 8.15

मामुपेत्य पुनर्जन्म दुःखालयमशाश्वतम्।
नाप्नुवन्ति महात्मानः संसिद्धिं परमां गताः।।

मुझे प्राप्त करके महात्मा पुनर्जन्म, जो दुख का स्थान और अस्थायी है, नहीं पाते; वे परम सिद्धि को प्राप्त कर चुके हैं।

Having attained Me, the great souls do not take rebirth, which is a temporary abode of sorrow; they attain supreme perfection.

Commentary: Krishna assures liberation from rebirth for devotees, offering hope and ultimate purpose.

Shloka 8.16

आब्रह्मभुवनाल्लोकाः पुनरावर्तिनोऽर्जुन।
मामुपेत्य तु कौन्तेय पुनर्जन्म न विद्यते।।

हे अर्जुन! ब्रह्मलोक तक सभी लोक पुनरावर्ती हैं। पर मुझे प्राप्त करके, हे कुन्तीपुत्र, पुनर्जन्म नहीं होता।

O Arjuna, all worlds up to Brahmaloka are subject to return. But having attained Me, O son of Kunti, there is no rebirth.

Commentary: Krishna contrasts temporary realms with eternal liberation, urging devotion to transcend cycles.

Shloka 8.17

सहस्रयुगपर्यन्तमहर्यद्ब्रह्मणो विदुः।
रात्रिं युगसहस्रान्तां तेऽहोरात्रविदो जनाः।।

जो लोग जानते हैं कि ब्रह्मा का एक दिन सहस्र युगों का और रात्रि भी सहस्र युगों की होती है, वे दिन-रात को जानने वाले हैं।

Those who know that Brahma’s day lasts a thousand yugas and his night a thousand yugas understand day and night.

Commentary: Krishna introduces cosmic cycles, emphasizing the vastness of divine time to inspire awe.

Shloka 8.18

अव्यक्ताद्व्यक्तयः सर्वाः प्रभवन्त्यहरागमे।
रात्र्यागमे प्रलीयन्ते तत्रैवाव्यक्तसंज्ञके।।

दिन के आगमन पर अव्यक्त से सभी व्यक्त प्राणी उत्पन्न होते हैं, और रात्रि के आगमन पर उसी अव्यक्त में लीन हो जाते हैं।

At the coming of day, all manifested beings arise from the unmanifest; at the coming of night, they dissolve into that unmanifest.

Commentary: Krishna describes the cyclic nature of creation, encouraging transcendence through devotion.

Shloka 8.21

अव्यक्तोऽक्षर इत्युक्तस्तमाहुः परमां गतिम्।
यं प्राप्य न निवर्तन्ते तद धाम परमं मम।।

जो अव्यक्त और अक्षर कहलाता है, उसे परम गति कहते हैं। उसे प्राप्त करके कोई वापस नहीं लौटता; वह मेरा परम धाम है।

That which is called the unmanifest and imperishable is said to be the supreme goal. Reaching it, none return; that is My supreme abode.

Commentary: Krishna describes the eternal Brahman, reinforcing liberation as the ultimate spiritual goal.

Shloka 8.23

यत्र काले त्वनावृत्तिमावृत्तिं चैव योगिनः।
प्रयाता यान्ति तं कालं वक्ष्यामि भरतर्षभ।।

हे भरतश्रेष्ठ! जिस काल में योगी अनावृत्ति और आवृत्ति को प्राप्त करते हैं, उस काल को मैं बताऊँगा।

O best of the Bharatas, I shall now declare the time when departing yogis attain non-return or return.

Commentary: Krishna introduces the two paths after death, setting the stage for explaining spiritual outcomes.

Shloka 8.24

अग्निर्ज्योतिरहः शुक्लः षण्मासा उत्तरायणम्।
तत्र प्रयाता गच्छन्ति ब्रह्म ब्रह्मविदो जनाः।।

अग्नि, ज्योति, दिन, शुक्ल पक्ष और उत्तरायण के छह महीनों में जो लोग देह त्यागते हैं, वे ब्रह्म को जानने वाले ब्रह्म को प्राप्त करते हैं।

Fire, light, daytime, the bright fortnight, the six months of the northern solstice—departing then, knowers of Brahman attain Brahman.

Commentary: Krishna describes the path of light leading to liberation, symbolizing spiritual readiness.

Shloka 8.26

शुक्लकृष्णे गती ह्येते जगतः शाश्वते मते।
एकया यात्यनावृत्तिमन्ययावर्तते पुनः।।

शुक्ल और कृष्ण मार्ग जगत के दो शाश्वत मार्ग माने जाते हैं। एक से अनावृत्ति होती है, और दूसरे से पुनर्जनन होता है।

The bright and dark paths are considered eternal in the world. By one, one attains non-return; by the other, one returns again.

Commentary: Krishna contrasts the paths of liberation and rebirth, urging spiritual practice to achieve the former.
