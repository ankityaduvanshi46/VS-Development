Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 5: Karma Sannyasa Yoga

Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 5: Karma Sannyasa Yoga

The Yoga of Renunciation of Action

Chapter Insights

Introduction

Chapter 5 of the Bhagavad Gita, known as Karma Sannyasa Yoga or the Yoga of Renunciation of Action, addresses Arjuna’s confusion about whether to pursue action (Karma Yoga) or renunciation (Sannyasa). Lord Krishna explains that both paths lead to liberation when practiced with wisdom, but Karma Yoga is more suitable for most, as it integrates action with detachment. This chapter emphasizes the unity of these paths, highlighting the importance of inner renunciation—detachment from ego and desire—over external withdrawal from duties.

By focusing on equanimity and self-realization, Chapter 5 offers practical guidance for living a balanced life while pursuing spiritual liberation, making it relevant for those navigating worldly responsibilities.

Historical and Philosophical Context

Rooted in the Mahabharata, Chapter 5 builds on the Upanishadic teachings of self-realization and integrates Karma Yoga and Jnana Yoga. Krishna reconciles the apparent conflict between action and renunciation, a central theme in Vedic philosophy. The chapter’s emphasis on equanimity (5.18) aligns with Advaita Vedanta’s non-dual perspective, while its practical approach to action resonates with the Bhagavata tradition. Set in the Kurukshetra war, it addresses ethical dilemmas in high-stakes contexts, offering timeless wisdom.

Key Themes

  • Unity of Paths: Karma Yoga and Sannyasa both lead to liberation when practiced with wisdom and detachment.
  • Inner Renunciation: True sannyasa is renouncing ego and desire, not merely physical action.
  • Equanimity: The wise see all beings equally, transcending distinctions like caste or species.
  • Self-Realization: Knowledge of the self as distinct from the body and mind leads to liberation.
  • Freedom from Karma: Actions performed with detachment do not bind the soul to material consequences.
  • Divine Connection: Surrendering actions to the divine fosters peace and spiritual growth.

Psychological Insights

Chapter 5’s focus on equanimity (5.19) aligns with mindfulness practices that reduce bias and promote emotional balance. The concept of detachment from results (5.12) mirrors cognitive behavioral techniques for managing stress and expectations. Krishna’s emphasis on self-realization (5.16) encourages self-awareness, fostering resilience and clarity in decision-making, applicable in modern psychological contexts.

Cross-Cultural Relevance

The chapter’s teachings on equanimity resonate with Buddhist principles of non-attachment and Christian ideals of universal love. The idea of selfless action parallels Stoic philosophy’s focus on duty without expectation. Krishna’s vision of seeing all beings equally (5.18) aligns with global humanistic values, making Chapter 5 universally relevant.

Philosophical Debates

Chapter 5 fuels debates about action versus renunciation. Some scholars view Karma Yoga as a practical alternative to monastic sannyasa, while others see it as a step toward Jnana Yoga. The chapter’s emphasis on equanimity raises questions about the nature of equality in spiritual and social contexts, enriching philosophical discourse.

Relevance Today

In 2025, Chapter 5’s teachings guide ethical decision-making in complex environments. Equanimity helps navigate polarized societies, while selfless action promotes integrity in professional and personal life. The focus on inner renunciation aids in managing distractions like social media, fostering mental clarity and purpose in addressing global challenges.

Commentators’ Perspectives

Adi Shankaracharya (8th Century)

Shankaracharya interprets Chapter 5 as emphasizing Jnana Yoga, with Karma Yoga as a means to purify the mind for self-realization. He views verses like 5.13 as pointing to the non-dual self, free from action.

Ramanujacharya (11th Century)

Ramanujacharya sees Chapter 5 as integrating Karma Yoga with devotion. He interprets selfless action (5.11) as service to God, aligning with Vishishtadvaita’s emphasis on surrender.

Mahatma Gandhi (20th Century)

Gandhi applied Chapter 5’s teachings to his non-violent movement, seeing verses like 5.12 as a call to act selflessly for societal good, maintaining detachment from outcomes.

Ethical Dilemmas and Resolutions

Arjuna’s Conflict

Arjuna is confused about choosing between action and renunciation. Krishna resolves this by explaining that both paths, when practiced with detachment, lead to liberation (5.2), encouraging Arjuna to act selflessly.

Modern Applications

Chapter 5 applies to dilemmas like balancing personal goals with social responsibilities. For instance, a professional can practice Karma Yoga by focusing on duty without attachment to promotions, fostering ethical leadership.

Meditation and Practice

Karma Yoga Practice

Perform daily tasks—work, volunteering, or caregiving—as offerings to a higher purpose, aligning with verses like 5.11, to cultivate detachment and purpose.

Meditation for Equanimity

Chapter 5’s focus on equanimity (5.18) supports mindfulness meditation, reflecting on the unity of all beings to foster compassion and reduce judgment.

Cultural Impact

Literature and Arts

Chapter 5 has influenced Indian art, with themes of equanimity depicted in classical dance and poetry. Its principles of selfless action appear in modern films and literature, emphasizing duty and detachment.

Global Influence

The chapter’s teachings on equanimity and selfless action have inspired global thinkers like Martin Luther King Jr., influencing civil rights movements and modern yoga practices.

Shlokas

Below is a selection of 20 key shlokas from Chapter 5, presented in Sanskrit with Hindi and English translations, accompanied by commentaries to highlight their significance.

Shloka 5.1

अर्जुन उवाच
संन्यासं कर्मणां कृष्ण पुनर्योगं च शंससि।
यच्छ्रेय एतयोरेकं तन्मे ब्रूहि सुनिश्चितम्।।

अर्जुन बोले: हे कृष्ण! तू कर्मों के संन्यास की और फिर योग की प्रशंसा करता है। इन दोनों में से जो श्रेष्ठ हो, वह मुझे निश्चित रूप से बता।

Arjuna said: O Krishna, You praise both renunciation of actions and Yoga. Tell me definitively which one is better.

Commentary: Arjuna’s question sets the stage for Krishna’s clarification on Karma Yoga and Sannyasa, addressing the core dilemma of action versus renunciation.

Shloka 5.2

श्री भगवानुवाच
संन्यासः कर्मयोगश्च निह्श्रेयसकरावुभौ।
तयोस्तु कर्मसंन्यासात्कर्मयोगो विशिष्यते।।

श्री भगवान बोले: संन्यास और कर्मयोग दोनों ही कल्याणकारी हैं, परन्तु कर्मसंन्यास से कर्मयोग श्रेष्ठ है।

The Blessed Lord said: Both renunciation and Karma Yoga lead to liberation, but of the two, Karma Yoga is superior to the renunciation of action.

Commentary: Krishna clarifies that Karma Yoga is more practical for most, as it integrates action with detachment, suitable for worldly life.

Shloka 5.3

ज्ञेयः स नित्यसंन्यासी यो न द्वेष्टि न काङ्क्षति।
निर्द्वन्द्वो हि महाबाहो सुखं बन्धात्प्रमुच्यते।।

वह व्यक्ति नित्य संन्यासी माना जाता है जो न द्वेष करता है, न कामना करता है। हे महाबाहो! द्वंद्वों से मुक्त होने के कारण वह आसानी से बंधन से मुक्त हो जाता है।

One who neither hates nor desires is known as a perpetual renunciant. O mighty-armed, being free from dualities, they are easily liberated from bondage.

Commentary: Krishna defines true renunciation as freedom from likes and dislikes, a practical approach to achieving inner peace.

Shloka 5.4

साङ्ख्ययोगौ पृथग्बालाः प्रवदन्ति न पण्डिताः।
एकमप्यास्थितः सम्यगुभयोर्विन्दति फलम्।।

बालक ही सांख्य और योग को पृथक् मानते हैं, पंडित नहीं। जो एक में भी ठीक से स्थित है, वह दोनों का फल प्राप्त करता है।

Only the ignorant speak of Sankhya and Yoga as distinct, not the wise. One who is properly established in either attains the fruit of both.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes the unity of knowledge and action, dispelling the notion of their separation, relevant for holistic spiritual practice.

Shloka 5.5

यत्साङ्ख्यैः प्राप्यते स्थानं तद्योगैरपि गम्यते।
एकं साङ्ख्यं च योगं च यः पश्यति स पश्यति।।

जो स्थान सांख्य से प्राप्त होता है, वही योग से भी प्राप्त होता है। जो सांख्य और योग को एक देखता है, वही सही देखता है।

The state attained by Sankhya is also reached by Yoga. One who sees Sankhya and Yoga as one truly sees.

Commentary: Krishna reinforces the equivalence of knowledge and action, encouraging a unified approach to liberation.

Shloka 5.6

संन्यासस्तु महाबाहो दुःखमाप्तुमयोगतः।
योगयुक्तो मुनिर्ब्रह्म नचिरेणाधिगच्छति।।

हे महाबाहो! योग के बिना संन्यास से दुःख ही प्राप्त होता है। योगयुक्त मुनि शीघ्र ही ब्रह्म को प्राप्त करता है।

O mighty-armed, renunciation is difficult to attain without Yoga. The sage engaged in Yoga quickly attains Brahman.

Commentary: Krishna highlights the necessity of Karma Yoga as a foundation for true renunciation, practical for those in active life.

Shloka 5.7

योगयुक्तो विशुद्धात्मा विजितात्मा जितेन्द्रियः।
सर्वभूतात्मभूतात्मा कुर्वन्नपि न लिप्यते।।

योग में युक्त, शुद्ध आत्मा वाला, आत्मा को जीतने वाला और इन्द्रियों को वश में करने वाला, जो सभी प्राणियों के आत्मा में अपने आत्मा को देखता है, वह कर्म करते हुए भी लिपटता नहीं।

One who is engaged in Yoga, pure in soul, master of the self, and conqueror of the senses, seeing the self in all beings, is not tainted by action.

Commentary: Krishna describes the ideal yogi, whose actions are free from bondage due to purity and universal vision, relevant for ethical conduct.

Shloka 5.8

नैव किञ्चित्करोमीति युक्तो मन्येत तत्त्ववित्।
पश्यञ्शृण्वन्स्पृशञ्जिघ्रन्नश्नन्गच्छन्स्वपञ्श्वसन्।।

तत्त्व को जानने वाला योगी यह माने कि मैं कुछ भी नहीं करता, यद्यपि वह देखता, सुनता, स्पर्श करता, सूंघता, खाता, चलता, सोता और श्वास लेता है।

A knower of truth, engaged in Yoga, thinks, “I do nothing at all,” though seeing, hearing, touching, smelling, eating, walking, sleeping, and breathing.

Commentary: Krishna explains the yogi’s detachment, realizing actions are performed by the body, not the self, fostering inner freedom.

Shloka 5.9

प्रलपन्विसृजन्गृह्णन्नुन्मिषन्निमिषन्नपि।
इन्द्रियाणीन्द्रियार्थेषु वर्तन्त इति धारयन्।।

बोलना, त्यागना, ग्रहण करना, आँखें खोलना और बंद करना—यह मानते हुए कि इन्द्रियाँ अपने विषयों में विचरण करती हैं।

Speaking, letting go, grasping, opening, and closing the eyes—believing that the senses operate upon their objects.

Commentary: Krishna continues the theme of detachment, emphasizing that the self is separate from sensory actions, aiding mindfulness.

Shloka 5.10

ब्रह्मण्याधाय कर्माणि सङ्गं त्यक्त्वा करोति यः।
लिप्यते न स पापेन पद्मपत्रमिवाम्भसा।।

जो कर्मों को ब्रह्म में अर्पित करके और आसक्ति त्यागकर कर्म करता है, वह पाप से नहीं लिपटता, जैसे कमल का पत्ता जल से।

One who performs actions, offering them to Brahman and abandoning attachment, is not tainted by sin, like a lotus leaf untouched by water.

Commentary: Krishna uses the lotus leaf metaphor to illustrate how selfless action avoids karmic bondage, practical for ethical living.

Shloka 5.11

कायेन मनसा बुद्ध्या केवलैरिन्द्रियैरपि।
योगिनः कर्म कुर्वन्ति सङ्गं त्यक्त्वाऽऽत्मशुद्धये।।

योगी शरीर, मन, बुद्धि और इन्द्रियों से कर्म करते हैं, आसक्ति त्यागकर, केवल आत्मा की शुद्धि के लिए।

Yogis perform actions with body, mind, intellect, and senses, without attachment, for the purification of the self.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes selfless action for spiritual purification, a principle applicable to modern altruistic endeavors.

Shloka 5.12

युक्तः कर्मफलं त्यक्त्वा शान्तिमाप्नोति नैष्ठिकीम्।
अयुक्तः कामकारेण फले सक्तो निबध्यते।।

योगी कर्मफल को त्यागकर परम शांति प्राप्त करता है, जबकि अयुक्त व्यक्ति कामना से प्रेरित होकर फल में आसक्त होकर बंधन में पड़ता है।

The disciplined yogi, renouncing the fruits of action, attains lasting peace. The undisciplined, attached to results, is bound by desire.

Commentary: Krishna contrasts the peace of detachment with the bondage of desire, offering a guide for stress-free living.

Shloka 5.13

सर्वकर्माणि मनसा संन्यास्यास्ते सुखं वशी।
नवद्वारे पुरे देही नैव कुर्वन्न कारयन्।।

सर्व कर्मों का मन से संन्यास करके, आत्मा को वश में करने वाला देही नौ द्वारों वाले पुर में सुखपूर्वक रहता है, न कुछ करता है, न करवाता है।

Renouncing all actions mentally, the self-controlled soul rests happily in the city of nine gates, neither acting nor causing action.

Commentary: Krishna uses the metaphor of the body as a city to describe inner renunciation, fostering a sense of inner peace.

Shloka 5.16

ज्ञानेन तु तदज्ञानं येषां नाशितमात्मनः।
तेषामादित्यवज्ज्ञानं प्रकाशति तत्परम्।।

जिनका अज्ञान आत्मज्ञान से नष्ट हो गया है, उनके लिए वह ज्ञान सूर्य के समान परम सत्य को प्रकाशित करता है।

For those whose ignorance is destroyed by self-knowledge, their knowledge, like the sun, reveals the Supreme.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes knowledge as a liberating force, illuminating the path to self-realization, relevant for personal growth.

Shloka 5.17

तद्बुद्धयस्तदात्मानस्तन्निष्ठास्तत्परायणाः।
गच्छन्त्यपुनरावृत्तिं ज्ञाननिर्धूतकल्मषाः।।

जिनकी बुद्धि उसमें स्थिर है, जिनका आत्मा उसमें लीन है, जो उसमें निष्ठा रखते हैं और जो उसे परम लक्ष्य मानते हैं, वे ज्ञान से पापमुक्त होकर पुनर्जन्म से मुक्त हो जाते हैं।

Those whose intellect is fixed on That, whose self is absorbed in That, who are steadfast in That, and whose supreme goal is That, their sins washed away by knowledge, attain liberation from rebirth.

Commentary: Krishna describes the path to liberation through focus on the divine, emphasizing knowledge as a purifying force.

Shloka 5.18

विद्याविनयसम्पन्ने ब्राह्मणे गवि हस्तिनि।
शुनि चैव श्वपाके च पण्डिताः समदर्शिनः।।

पंडित पुरुष विद्या और विनय से युक्त ब्राह्मण, गाय, हाथी, कुत्ते और चांडाल में भी समान दृष्टि रखते हैं।

The wise see with equal vision a learned and humble Brahmin, a cow, an elephant, a dog, and an outcaste.

Commentary: Krishna’s vision of equanimity transcends social and species distinctions, promoting universal compassion and equality.

Shloka 5.19

इहैव तैर्जितः सर्गो येषां साम्ये स्थितं मनः।
निर्दोषं हि समं ब्रह्म तस्माद्ब्रह्मणि ते स्थिताः।।

जिनका मन साम्य में स्थिर है, उन्होंने यहीं संसार को जीत लिया है। ब्रह्म निर्दोष और सम है, इसलिए वे ब्रह्म में स्थित हैं।

Those whose minds are established in equality have conquered the cycle of birth and death in this very life. Brahman is flawless and equal; thus, they are established in Brahman.

Commentary: Krishna links equanimity to liberation, offering a practical path for transcending worldly suffering through balanced vision.

Shloka 5.20

न प्रहृष्येत्प्रियं प्राप्य नोद्विजेत्प्राप्य चाप्रियम्।
स्थिरबुद्धिरसम्मूढो ब्रह्मविद्ब्रह्मणि स्थितः।।

जो प्रिय प्राप्त होने पर हर्षित नहीं होता और अप्रिय प्राप्त होने पर उद्विग्न नहीं होता, वह स्थिरबुद्धि, असम्मूढ़, ब्रह्म को जानने वाला ब्रह्म में स्थित है।

One who does not rejoice in the pleasant nor grieve in the unpleasant, with steady intellect and undeluded, knowing Brahman, is established in Brahman.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes emotional stability as a sign of self-realization, relevant for maintaining calm in life’s ups and downs.

Shloka 5.25

लभन्ते ब्रह्मनिर्वाणमृषयः क्षीणकल्मषाः।
छिन्नद्वैधा यतात्मानः सर्वभूतहिते रताः।।

वे ऋषि जिनके पाप नष्ट हो गए हैं, जिनके द्वंद्व कट गए हैं, जो आत्मसंयमी हैं और जो सभी प्राणियों के हित में रत हैं, वे ब्रह्मनिर्वाण को प्राप्त करते हैं।

Those sages whose sins are destroyed, whose dualities are cut asunder, who are self-controlled and devoted to the welfare of all beings, attain Brahman’s liberation.

Commentary: Krishna describes the qualities of a liberated sage, emphasizing compassion and self-control as paths to liberation.

Shloka 5.29

भोक्तारं यज्ञतपसां सर्वलोकमहेश्वरम्।
सुहृदं सर्वभूतानां ज्ञात्वा मां शान्तिमृच्छति।।

मुझे यज्ञ और तप का भोक्ता, सभी लोकों का महान ईश्वर और सभी प्राणियों का सुहृद (मित्र) जानकर, वह शांति प्राप्त करता है।

Knowing Me as the enjoyer of sacrifices and austerities, the great Lord of all worlds, and the friend of all beings, one attains peace.

Commentary: Krishna concludes by emphasizing devotion and knowledge of the divine as the ultimate source of peace, integrating Bhakti Yoga.
