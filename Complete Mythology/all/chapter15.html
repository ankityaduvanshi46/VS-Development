Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 15: Purushottama Yoga

Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 15: Purushottama Yoga

The Yoga of the Supreme Person

Chapter Insights

Introduction

Chapter 15 of the Bhagavad Gita, known as Purushottama Yoga or The Yoga of the Supreme Person, presents a profound metaphysical framework. Lord Krishna describes the cosmic tree of material existence, the nature of the individual soul, and the Supreme Person (Purushottama), who transcends both the perishable and imperishable. This chapter emphasizes detachment from the material world and devotion to Krishna as the path to liberation. Its concise yet comprehensive teachings make it a pivotal guide for spiritual seekers.

Chapter 15’s vision of transcending material entanglement through divine connection remains highly relevant for finding purpose in today’s complex world.

Historical and Philosophical Context

Rooted in the Mahabharata, Chapter 15 integrates Upanishadic concepts of the cosmic tree (15.1-3) and Sankhya’s distinction between Kshara (perishable) and Akshara (imperishable), culminating in the Purushottama (15.18). Krishna’s teachings address Arjuna’s need for clarity on liberation amidst his warrior duties, blending Jnana and Bhakti Yoga.

Key Themes

  • Cosmic Tree Metaphor: Material existence is likened to an inverted tree, rooted in the divine but entangled in illusion.
  • Three Purushas: The perishable (Kshara), imperishable (Akshara), and supreme person (Purushottama) define existence.
  • Detachment for Liberation: Cutting the tree of attachment with knowledge leads to freedom.
  • Supreme Person: Krishna as Purushottama is the ultimate reality, transcending matter and soul.
  • Devotion’s Role: Knowing and surrendering to Krishna ensures liberation from samsara.
  • Divine Presence: Krishna sustains all existence, from the sun to the soul’s vitality.

Psychological Insights

The cosmic tree metaphor (15.1-3) encourages detaching from material desires, aligning with mindfulness practices to reduce anxiety. Recognizing Krishna as the source of consciousness (15.13) fosters a sense of purpose and stability. The emphasis on discernment (15.16-18) promotes clarity, reducing ego-driven conflicts and enhancing mental resilience.

Cross-Cultural Relevance

The concept of a supreme reality (15.18) resonates with monotheistic traditions like Christianity and Islam, while the cosmic tree parallels mythological symbols in other cultures. The call for detachment (15.3) aligns with Buddhist and Stoic teachings, making Chapter 15 universally applicable.

Philosophical Debates

Chapter 15 raises questions about the nature of reality—material, spiritual, and supreme (15.16-18)—fueling debates on dualism versus non-dualism. The role of devotion versus knowledge in liberation (15.19) enriches discourse on spiritual paths.

Relevance Today

In 2025, Chapter 15’s teachings inspire detachment from consumerism and digital distractions, promoting inner peace. Its vision of Krishna as the sustainer (15.13-14) fosters environmental consciousness and gratitude, addressing modern challenges like materialism and disconnection.

Commentators’ Perspectives

Adi Shankaracharya (8th Century)

Shankaracharya interprets Chapter 15 as a guide to non-dual realization, viewing the Purushottama (15.18) as Brahman, with detachment (15.3) leading to unity with the divine.

Ramanujacharya (11th Century)

Ramanujacharya sees Chapter 15 as emphasizing devotion to Krishna as the Purushottama (15.18), with surrender (15.19) central to Vishishtadvaita liberation.

Mahatma Gandhi (20th Century)

Gandhi drew on Chapter 15’s call for detachment (15.3) and devotion (15.19) to guide his non-violent resistance, seeing Krishna’s universal presence in all beings.

Ethical Dilemmas and Resolutions

Arjuna’s Conflict

Arjuna struggles with attachment to worldly roles. Krishna resolves this by teaching detachment from the cosmic tree (15.3) and devotion to the Purushottama (15.19), enabling action without bondage.

Modern Applications

Chapter 15 applies to dilemmas like balancing ambition with inner peace. Practicing detachment (15.3) in professional life fosters ethical decisions and reduces stress.

Meditation and Practice

Jnana Yoga Practice

Reflect on the cosmic tree (15.1-3) and the Purushottama (15.18) through self-inquiry, cultivating detachment and awareness of the supreme reality.

Bhakti Yoga Practice

Meditate on Krishna as the sustainer of all (15.13-14), offering actions to him to foster devotion and transcend material attachment.

Cultural Impact

Literature and Arts

Chapter 15’s cosmic tree imagery has inspired Indian art, literature, and temple iconography, with its themes reflected in devotional poetry and music.

Global Influence

The chapter’s teachings on the supreme reality have shaped global Vedanta movements and interfaith dialogues, promoting universal spirituality and transcendence.

Shlokas

Below is a selection of all 20 shlokas from Chapter 15, presented in Sanskrit with Hindi and English translations, accompanied by commentaries to highlight their significance.

Shloka 15.1

श्री भगवानुवाच
ऊर्ध्वमूलमधःशाखमश्वत्थं प्राहुरव्ययम्।
छन्दांसि यस्य पर्णानि यस्तं वेद स वेदवित्।।

श्री भगवान बोले: ऊर्ध्वमूल और अधःशाखा वाला, अव्यय अश्वत्थ वृक्ष कहलाता है, जिसके पर्ण वेद हैं। जो इसे जानता है, वह वेदवित है।

The Blessed Lord said: They speak of an imperishable banyan tree with roots above and branches below, whose leaves are the Vedas. One who knows this knows the Vedas.

Commentary: Krishna introduces the cosmic tree metaphor, symbolizing material existence rooted in the divine but entangled in illusion.

Shloka 15.2

अधश्चोर्ध्वं प्रसृतास्तस्य शाखाः गुणप्रवृद्धा विषयप्रवालाः।
अधश्च मूलान्यनुसन्ततानि कर्मानुबन्धीनि मनुष्यलोके।।

इसकी शाखाएँ गुणों से पुष्ट होकर विषयों रूपी कोंपलों सहित नीचे और ऊपर फैली हैं। नीचे इसकी जड़ें कर्मों के बंधनों से मनुष्यलोक में विस्तृत हैं।

Its branches spread below and above, nourished by the gunas, with sense objects as sprouts. Its roots extend downward, binding through actions in the human world.

Commentary: Krishna describes the tree’s entanglement in material desires and actions, urging detachment.

Shloka 15.3

न रूपमस्येह तथोपलभ्यते नान्तो न चादिर्न च संप्रतिष्ठा।
अश्वत्थमेनं सुविरूढमूलमसङ्गशस्त्रेण दृढेन छित्त्वा।।

इसका रूप यहाँ ऐसा नहीं दिखता, न इसका अंत है, न आदि, न स्थिरता। इस सुविरूढ़ मूल वाले अश्वत्थ को वैराग्य रूपी मजबूत शस्त्र से काटकर।

Its form is not perceived here, nor its end, beginning, or foundation. Having cut this firm-rooted banyan tree with the strong axe of detachment.

Commentary: Krishna advises cutting the tree of attachment with detachment to transcend material bondage.

Shloka 15.4

ततः पदं तत्परिमार्गितव्यं यत्र गता न निवर्तन्ति भूयः।
तमेव चाद्यं पुरुषं प्रपद्ये यतः प्रवृत्तिः प्रसृता पुराणी।।

तत्पश्चात् उस पद का अन्वेषण करना चाहिए, जहाँ जाकर लोग फिर नहीं लौटते। मैं उसी आदि पुरुष की शरण लेता हूँ, जिससे यह पुरानी सृष्टि प्रसृत हुई।

Thereafter, one must seek that state from which there is no return. I surrender to that primeval Person from whom this ancient creation has sprung.

Commentary: Krishna points to the supreme destination, attainable through surrender to the divine source.

Shloka 15.5

निर्मानमोहा जितसङ्गदोषा अध्यात्मनित्या विनिवृत्तकामाः।
द्वन्द्वैर्विमुक्ताः सुखदुःखसंज्ञैर्गच्छन्त्यमूढाः पदमव्ययं तत्।।

निर्मान, मोह से मुक्त, संगदोष को जीतने वाले, अध्यात्म में नित्य, कामनाओं से निवृत्त, सुख-दुख रूपी द्वंद्वों से मुक्त, अमूढ लोग उस अव्यय पद को प्राप्त करते हैं।

Free from pride and delusion, victorious over attachment, constant in self-knowledge, desireless, liberated from dualities of pleasure and pain, the undeluded reach that imperishable state.

Commentary: Krishna describes the qualities of those who attain liberation, emphasizing detachment and wisdom.

Shloka 15.6

न तद्भासति सूर्यो न शशाङ्को न पावकः।
यद्गत्वा न निवर्तन्ते तद्धाम परमं मम।।

न सूर्य, न चन्द्र, न अग्नि वहाँ प्रकाश करते हैं। जहाँ जाकर लोग नहीं लौटते, वह मेरा परम धाम है।

Neither the sun, moon, nor fire illumines it. Having gone there, none return—that is My supreme abode.

Commentary: Krishna describes his transcendental abode, beyond material illumination, as the ultimate destination.

Shloka 15.7

ममैवांशो जीवलोके जीवभूतः सनातनः।
मनःषष्ठानीन्द्रियाणि प्रकृतिस्थानि कर्षति।।

जीवलोक में जीव मेरा ही सनातन अंश है। वह प्रकृति में स्थित मन सहित छह इन्द्रियों को आकर्षित करता है।

An eternal fragment of Myself becomes the living soul in the world of beings, drawing to itself the six senses, including the mind, rooted in Prakriti.

Commentary: Krishna explains the soul as his eternal fragment, entangled in material senses, urging recognition of its divine origin.

Shloka 15.8

शरीरं यदवाप्नोति यच्चाप्युत्क्रामतीश्वरः।
गृहीत्वैतानि संयाति वायुर्गन्धानिवाशयात्।।

जब जीव शरीर प्राप्त करता है और जब उसका त्याग करता है, तब वह इन इन्द्रियों को लेकर जाता है, जैसे वायु गंध को ले जाती है।

When the soul acquires a body or departs from it, it takes these senses along, as the wind carries fragrances from their source.

Commentary: Krishna illustrates the soul’s transmigration, emphasizing its distinctness from the body.

Shloka 15.9

श्रोत्रं चक्षुः स्पर्शनं च रसनं घ्राणमेव च।
अधिष्ठाय मनश्चायं विषयानुपसेवति।।

वह श्रोत्र, चक्षु, स्पर्श, रसना और घ्राण सहित मन को अधिष्ठान करके विषयों का उपभोग करता है।

Presiding over the ear, eye, touch, taste, smell, and mind, it enjoys the objects of the senses.

Commentary: Krishna describes the soul’s interaction with senses, highlighting its role as the experiencer.

Shloka 15.10

उत्क्रामन्तं स्थितं वापि भुञ्जानं वा गुणान्वितम्।
विमूढा नानुपश्यन्ति पश्यन्ति ज्ञानचक्षुषः।।

उत्क्रान्ति, स्थिति या गुणों से युक्त भोग करने वाले को मूढ़ नहीं देखते, ज्ञानचक्षु वाले ही देखते हैं।

The deluded do not see it departing, abiding, or enjoying with the gunas; those with the eye of knowledge see it.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes that only the wise perceive the soul’s eternal nature beyond material activities.

Shloka 15.11

यतन्तो योगिनश्चैनं पश्यन्त्यात्मन्यवस्थितम्।
यतन्तोऽप्यकृतात्मानो नैनं पश्यन्त्यचेतसः।।

योगी लोग यत्न करके इसे आत्मा में स्थित देखते हैं, किन्तु अकृत आत्मा वाले मूर्ख यत्न करने पर भी इसे नहीं देखते।

Striving yogis see it established in the self, but the unrefined and unintelligent, though striving, do not see it.

Commentary: Krishna underscores the need for disciplined effort and purity to perceive the soul.

Shloka 15.12

यदादित्यगतं तेजो जगद्भासति सर्वशः।
यच्चन्द्रमसि यच्चाग्नौ तत्तेजो विद्धि मम।।

जो सूर्य में तेज है, जो विश्व को प्रकाशित करता है, जो चन्द्रमा और अग्नि में है, वह तेज मेरा जान।

The light in the sun that illumines the world, that in the moon and fire—know that light to be Mine.

Commentary: Krishna identifies himself as the source of all light, inspiring awe for his universal presence.

Shloka 15.13

गामाविश्य च भूतानि धारयाम्यहमोजसा।
पुष्णामि चौषधीः सर्वाः सोमो भूत्वा रसात्मकः।।

मैं ओज से पृथ्वी में प्रवेश करके सभी प्राणियों को धारण करता हूँ और रसात्मक सोम बनकर सभी औषधियों को पुष्ट करता हूँ।

Entering the earth, I sustain all beings with My energy; becoming the essence of Soma, I nourish all herbs.

Commentary: Krishna’s role as the sustainer of life fosters reverence for his divine presence in nature.

Shloka 15.14

अहं वैश्वानरो भूत्वा प्राणिनां देहमाश्रितः।
प्राणापानसमायुक्तः पचाम्यन्नं चतुर्विधम्।।

मैं वैश्वानर अग्नि बनकर प्राणियों के शरीर में निवास करता हूँ और प्राण-अपान से संयुक्त होकर चार प्रकार के अन्न को पचाता हूँ।

Becoming the digestive fire, I reside in the bodies of beings; united with Prana and Apana, I digest the four kinds of food.

Commentary: Krishna’s presence in bodily functions highlights his intimate role in sustaining life.

Shloka 15.15

सर्वस्य चाहं हृदि सन्निविष्टो मत्तः स्मृतिर्ज्ञानमपोहनं च।
वेदैश्च सर्वैरहमेव वेद्यो वेदान्तकृद्वेदविदेव चाहम्।।

मैं सभी के हृदय में निवास करता हूँ; मुझसे स्मृति, ज्ञान और अपोहन होता है। मैं ही सभी वेदों से जानने योग्य हूँ, वेदान्त का कर्ता और वेदवित् हूँ।

I am seated in the hearts of all; from Me come memory, knowledge, and their loss. I am the knowable of all Vedas, the author of Vedanta, and the knower of the Vedas.

Commentary: Krishna’s role as the source of consciousness and wisdom inspires devotion and self-realization.

Shloka 15.16

द्वाविमौ पुरुषौ लोके क्षरश्चाक्षर एव च।
क्षरः सर्वं भूतानि कूटस्थोऽक्षर उच्यते।।

इस लोक में दो पुरुष हैं—क्षर और अक्षर। सभी भूत क्षर हैं, और कूटस्थ को अक्षर कहा जाता है।

There are two beings in this world: the perishable and the imperishable. All beings are perishable; the immutable is called imperishable.

Commentary: Krishna introduces the distinction between the perishable body and imperishable soul, setting the stage for the supreme.

Shloka 15.17

उत्तमः पुरुषस्त्वन्यः परमात्मेत्युदाहृतः।
यो लोकत्रयमाविश्य बिभर्त्यव्यय ईश्वरः।।

इससे भिन्न उत्तम पुरुष परमात्मा कहलाता है, जो तीनों लोकों में प्रवेश करके अव्यय ईश्वर के रूप में सबको धारण करता है।

But distinct is the Supreme Person, called the Supreme Self, the imperishable Lord who pervades and sustains the three worlds.

Commentary: Krishna introduces the Purushottama, transcending both perishable and imperishable, as the ultimate reality.

Shloka 15.18

यस्मात्क्षरमतीतोऽहमक्षरादपि चोत्तमः।
अतोऽस्मि लोके वेदे च प्रथितः पुरुषोत्तमः।।

क्योंकि मैं क्षर से अतीत और अक्षर से भी उत्तम हूँ, इसलिए लोक और वेद में पुरुषोत्तम के रूप में प्रसिद्ध हूँ।

As I transcend the perishable and am above the imperishable, I am renowned in the world and Vedas as the Supreme Person.

Commentary: Krishna declares himself the Purushottama, inspiring devotion to the ultimate reality.

Shloka 15.19

यो मां एवमसम्मूढो जानाति पुरुषोत्तमम्।
स सर्वविद्भजति मां सर्वभावेन भारत।।

जो असम्मूढ होकर मुझे पुरुषोत्तम जानता है, वह सर्वज्ञ होकर सभी भावों से मुझे भजता है, हे भारत।

One who, undeluded, knows Me as the Supreme Person, knows all and worships Me with all their being, O Bharata.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes that knowing him as Purushottama leads to complete devotion and liberation.

Shloka 15.20

इति गुह्यतमं शास्त्रं मया तेऽद्योदितं स्मृतम्।
एतद्बुद्ध्वा बुद्धिमान्स्यात्कृतकृत्यश्च भारत।।

इस प्रकार यह अति गोपनीय शास्त्र मुझसे तुझको कहा गया। इसे जानकर बुद्धिमान और कृतकृत्य हो जाता है, हे भारत।

Thus, the most secret teaching has been imparted by Me. Knowing this, one becomes wise and fulfilled, O Bharata.

Commentary: Krishna concludes Chapter 15, affirming that this knowledge leads to wisdom and spiritual fulfillment.
