Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 6: Dhyana Yoga

Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 6: Dhyana Yoga

The Yoga of Meditation

Chapter Insights

Introduction

Chapter 6 of the Bhagavad Gita, known as Dhyana Yoga or the Yoga of Meditation, delves into the practice of meditation as a means to achieve self-realization and liberation. Lord Krishna explains the qualities of a true yogi, the techniques of meditation, and the importance of discipline and detachment. This chapter builds on the concepts of Karma Yoga and Jnana Yoga from previous chapters, presenting meditation as a practical tool to control the mind and attain inner peace.

By emphasizing mental discipline and equanimity, Chapter 6 offers a guide for spiritual seekers to cultivate focus and transcend worldly distractions, making it highly relevant for modern mindfulness practices.

Historical and Philosophical Context

Rooted in the Mahabharata, Chapter 6 draws from the Upanishads and Patanjali’s Yoga Sutras, emphasizing meditation as a path to self-realization. Krishna integrates Karma Yoga’s selfless action with Dhyana Yoga’s mental discipline, aligning with the Vedic tradition of balancing action and contemplation. The chapter’s focus on the self as the ultimate friend or enemy (6.5) reflects Advaita Vedanta’s non-dual philosophy, while its practical meditation techniques make it accessible to all spiritual aspirants.

Key Themes

  • Meditation as a Path: Focused meditation leads to control of the mind and realization of the self.
  • Self-Discipline: The self is both friend and enemy, requiring discipline to overcome lower tendencies.
  • Equanimity: A yogi remains balanced in success and failure, pleasure and pain.
  • Detachment: Renouncing desires and attachments is essential for meditative success.
  • Practical Meditation: Krishna provides specific techniques, such as posture and focus, for effective meditation.
  • Universal Vision: The yogi sees the divine in all beings, fostering compassion and unity.

Psychological Insights

Chapter 6’s emphasis on mind control (6.16) aligns with modern mindfulness and cognitive psychology, promoting focus and emotional regulation. The concept of the self as friend or enemy (6.5) mirrors self-efficacy theories, encouraging personal responsibility for mental states. Krishna’s meditation techniques (6.11-13) resemble mindfulness practices, reducing stress and enhancing mental clarity in today’s fast-paced world.

Cross-Cultural Relevance

The chapter’s meditation practices resonate with Buddhist mindfulness and Zen practices, as well as Christian contemplative traditions like centering prayer. The emphasis on equanimity (6.9) parallels Stoic philosophy’s focus on emotional resilience. The universal vision of seeing the divine in all (6.30) aligns with global humanistic values, making Chapter 6 widely applicable.

Philosophical Debates

Chapter 6 sparks discussions about the role of meditation versus action in spiritual life. Some interpret Dhyana Yoga as superior to Karma Yoga, while others see it as complementary. The chapter’s focus on self-discipline raises questions about free will versus destiny, enriching philosophical discourse on personal agency.

Relevance Today

In 2025, Chapter 6’s meditation techniques are vital for managing stress from digital overload and societal pressures. Its emphasis on equanimity helps navigate polarized environments, while self-discipline fosters resilience in addressing challenges like mental health or global crises. The chapter’s universal vision promotes inclusivity and compassion in diverse societies.

Commentators’ Perspectives

Adi Shankaracharya (8th Century)

Shankaracharya views Chapter 6 as a guide to meditation for realizing the non-dual self. He interprets verses like 6.20 as pointing to the state of samadhi, where the self merges with Brahman.

Ramanujacharya (11th Century)

Ramanujacharya sees Dhyana Yoga as a means to deepen devotion to God. He interprets meditation (6.14) as focusing on Krishna, aligning with Vishishtadvaita’s emphasis on divine connection.

Mahatma Gandhi (20th Century)

Gandhi applied Chapter 6’s teachings on self-discipline and equanimity to his non-violent resistance, using meditation to maintain focus and inner peace during India’s freedom struggle.

Ethical Dilemmas and Resolutions

Arjuna’s Conflict

Arjuna questions how to control the restless mind. Krishna resolves this by teaching meditation techniques (6.11-14) and emphasizing gradual practice with discipline (6.36), enabling Arjuna to fulfill his duties with clarity.

Modern Applications

Chapter 6 applies to dilemmas like managing stress or distractions. For example, a professional can use meditation to stay focused amidst workplace pressures, aligning actions with higher purpose.

Meditation and Practice

Dhyana Yoga Practice

Practice meditation as outlined in 6.11-14: sit in a clean, quiet place, focus on a single point, and regulate breath to calm the mind, fostering self-awareness.

Mindfulness for Equanimity

Chapter 6’s emphasis on equanimity (6.9) supports daily mindfulness practices, such as observing thoughts without attachment, to cultivate balance and compassion.

Cultural Impact

Literature and Arts

Chapter 6 has inspired Indian art, with meditation depicted in classical dance and yoga iconography. Its themes of discipline influence modern literature and films on spiritual journeys.

Global Influence

The chapter’s meditation techniques have shaped global yoga and mindfulness movements, influencing thinkers like Jon Kabat-Zinn and modern wellness practices.

Shlokas

Below is a selection of 20 key shlokas from Chapter 6, presented in Sanskrit with Hindi and English translations, accompanied by commentaries to highlight their significance.

Shloka 6.1

श्री भगवानुवाच
अनाश्रितः कर्मफलं कार्यं कर्म करोति यः।
स संन्यासी च योगी च न निरग्निर्न चाक्रियः।।

श्री भगवान बोले: जो कर्मफल की आशा नहीं करता और कर्तव्य कर्म करता है, वही संन्यासी और योगी है, न कि वह जो अग्नि त्याग दे या कर्म न करे।

The Blessed Lord said: One who performs obligatory actions without depending on their fruits is a true renunciant and yogi, not one who renounces fire or action.

Commentary: Krishna redefines renunciation as selfless action, setting the stage for integrating Karma Yoga with meditation.

Shloka 6.2

यं संन्यासमिति प्राहुर्योगं तं विद्धि पाण्डव।
न ह्यसंन्यस्तसङ्कल्पो योगी भवति कश्चन।।

हे पाण्डव! जिसे संन्यास कहते हैं, उसे योग समझ। क्योंकि बिना संकल्प त्यागे कोई योगी नहीं हो सकता।

O Pandava, know that what is called renunciation is Yoga. No one becomes a yogi without renouncing selfish intentions.

Commentary: Krishna equates renunciation with Yoga, emphasizing mental detachment as essential for spiritual practice.

Shloka 6.3

आरुरुक्षोर्मुनेर्योगं कर्म कारणमुच्यते।
योगारूढस्य तस्यैव शमः कारणमुच्यते।।

योग में प्रवेश करने की इच्छा रखने वाले मुनि के लिए कर्म कारण कहा जाता है। योग में सिद्ध होने पर उसी के लिए शांति कारण कही जाती है।

For a sage desiring to attain Yoga, action is the means. For one established in Yoga, tranquility is the means.

Commentary: Krishna outlines a progression from action to meditation, guiding beginners and advanced practitioners alike.

Shloka 6.5

उद्धरेदात्मनात्मानं नात्मानमवसादयेत्।
आत्मैव ह्यात्मनो बन्धुरात्मैव रिपुरात्मनः।।

आत्मा से आत्मा को उद्धार करना चाहिए, आत्मा को नीचे नहीं गिराना चाहिए। आत्मा ही आत्मा का मित्र है और आत्मा ही आत्मा का शत्रु।

One should uplift oneself by the self and not degrade oneself. The self alone is one’s friend, and the self alone is one’s enemy.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes self-discipline, highlighting personal responsibility for spiritual growth, relevant for self-improvement.

Shloka 6.6

बन्धुरात्मात्मनस्तस्य येनात्मैवात्मना जितः।
अनात्मनस्तु शत्रुत्वे वर्तेतात्मैव शत्रुवत्।।

जिसने आत्मा से आत्मा को जीत लिया, उसके लिए आत्मा मित्र है। पर जिसने आत्मा को नहीं जीता, उसके लिए आत्मा शत्रु की तरह व्यवहार करता है।

The self is a friend to one who has conquered it by the self. For one who has not conquered it, the self acts as an enemy.

Commentary: Krishna stresses mastering the mind, a principle applicable to overcoming negative habits in modern life.

Shloka 6.7

जितात्मनः प्रशान्तस्य परमात्मा समाहितः।
शीतोष्णसुखदुःखेषु तथ मानावमानयोः।।

जिसने आत्मा को जीत लिया और जो शांत है, उसका परमात्मा समाहित है। वह शीत-उष्ण, सुख-दुख और मान-अपमान में समान रहता है।

For one who has conquered the self and is tranquil, the Supreme Self is steady, in cold and heat, pleasure and pain, honor and dishonor.

Commentary: Krishna describes the equanimity of a realized yogi, offering a model for emotional resilience in challenging situations.

Shloka 6.9

सुहृन्मित्रार्युदासीनमध्यस्थद्वेष्यबन्धुषु।
साधुष्वपि च पापेषु समबुद्धिर्विशिष्यते।।

जो मित्र, शत्रु, उदासीन, मध्यस्थ, द्वेष्य, बंधु, साधु और पापी में समबुद्धि रखता है, वह विशेष रूप से श्रेष्ठ है।

One who has equal vision toward friend, foe, neutral, mediator, the hateful, the relative, the virtuous, and the sinful excels.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes universal equanimity, promoting compassion and fairness, relevant for fostering inclusivity.

Shloka 6.10

योगी युञ्जीत सततमात्मानं रहसि स्थितः।
एकाकी यतचित्तात्मा निराशीरपरिग्रहः।।

योगी एकांत में रहकर, मन और आत्मा को संयमित करके, आशा और परिग्रह से मुक्त होकर, सदा आत्मा को योग में युक्त करे।

A yogi should constantly engage the self in Yoga, dwelling in solitude, with mind and self controlled, free from desire and possessiveness.

Commentary: Krishna outlines the conditions for meditation, emphasizing solitude and detachment, practical for modern mindfulness.

Shloka 6.11

शुचौ देशे प्रतिष्ठाप्य स्थिरमासनमात्मनः।
नात्युच्छ्रितं नातिनीचं चैलाजिनकुशोत्तरम्।।

शुद्ध स्थान में, न बहुत ऊँचा और न बहुत नीचा, कुशा, मृगचर्म और वस्त्र से बना स्थिर आसन स्थापित करके।

In a clean place, establishing a steady seat for oneself, neither too high nor too low, covered with cloth, deerskin, and kusha grass.

Commentary: Krishna provides practical meditation instructions, emphasizing a conducive environment, relevant for modern meditation setups.

Shloka 6.12

तत्रैकाग्रं मनः कृत्वा यतचित्तेन्द्रियक्रियः।
उपविश्यासने युञ्ज्याद्योगमात्मविशुद्धये।।

वहाँ मन को एकाग्र करके, चित्त और इन्द्रियों की क्रिया को संयमित करके, आसन पर बैठकर आत्मा की शुद्धि के लिए योग में युक्त हो।

There, focusing the mind, controlling the activities of mind and senses, seated on the seat, one should practice Yoga for self-purification.

Commentary: Krishna details the meditation process, emphasizing focus and discipline, aligning with mindfulness practices.

Shloka 6.13

समं कायशिरोग्रीवं धारयन्नचलं स्थिरः।
सम्प्रेक्ष्य नासिकाग्रं स्वं दिशश्चानवलोकयन्।।

शरीर, सिर और ग्रीवा को समान और स्थिर रखकर, नासिका के अग्रभाग को देखते हुए, अन्य दिशाओं में न देखते हुए।

Holding the body, head, and neck erect and steady, gazing at the tip of the nose, not looking around.

Commentary: Krishna provides specific posture and focus techniques for meditation, practical for achieving mental clarity.

Shloka 6.14

प्रशान्तात्मा विगतभीर्ब्रह्मचारिव्रते स्थितः।
मनः संयम्य मच्चित्तो युक्त आसीत मत्परः।।

शांत आत्मा, भयमुक्त, ब्रह्मचर्य व्रत में स्थिर, मन को संयमित करके, मुझमें चित्त लगाकर, मुझमें समर्पित होकर युक्त रहे।

With a tranquil soul, free from fear, firm in the vow of celibacy, controlling the mind, thinking of Me, devoted to Me, one should sit in Yoga.

Commentary: Krishna integrates devotion into meditation, emphasizing purity and focus on the divine for spiritual success.

Shloka 6.16

नात्यश्नतस्तु योगोऽस्ति न चैकान्तमनश्नतः।
न चातिस्वप्नशीलस्य जाग्रतो नैव चार्जुन।।

हे अर्जुन! न बहुत खाने वाले का, न बिल्कुल न खाने वाले का, न बहुत सोने वाले का, और न बिल्कुल न सोने वाले का योग होता है।

O Arjuna, Yoga is not for one who eats too much or too little, nor for one who sleeps too much or too little.

Commentary: Krishna advocates moderation in lifestyle, a practical principle for maintaining balance in meditation and daily life.

Shloka 6.17

युक्ताहारविहारस्य युक्तचेष्टस्य कर्मसु।
युक्तस्वप्नावबोधस्य योगो भवति दुःखहा।।

जो खाने-पीने, विचरण, कर्म और सोने-जागने में संयमित है, उसके लिए योग दुखनाशक होता है।

For one who is moderate in eating and recreation, disciplined in action, and regulated in sleep and wakefulness, Yoga destroys sorrow.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes a balanced lifestyle as essential for Yoga, relevant for modern wellness practices.

Shloka 6.20

यत्रोपरमते चित्तं निरुद्धं योगसेवया।
यत्र चैवात्मनात्मानं पश्यन्नात्मनि तुष्यति।।

जहाँ योग के अभ्यास से चित्त निरुद्ध होकर शांत हो जाता है, और जहाँ आत्मा द्वारा आत्मा को देखकर आत्मा में संतुष्ट होता है।

When the mind, restrained by the practice of Yoga, becomes still, and seeing the self by the self, one is satisfied in the self.

Commentary: Krishna describes the state of samadhi, where meditation leads to self-realization and inner contentment.

Shloka 6.23

तं विद्याद् दुःखसंयोगवियोगं योगसंज्ञितम्।
स निश्चयेन योक्तव्यो योगोऽनिर्विण्णचेतसा।।

उसे दुख के संयोग से वियोग वाला योग जानना चाहिए। इसे निश्चयपूर्वक और उदासीन चित्त से करना चाहिए।

Know that Yoga, which is severance from contact with sorrow, should be practiced with determination and a non-despondent mind.

Commentary: Krishna defines Yoga as liberation from suffering, emphasizing perseverance in practice, relevant for resilience.

Shloka 6.26

यतो यतो निश्चरति मनश्चञ्चलमस्थिरम्।
ततस्ततो नियम्यैतदात्मन्येव वशं नयेत्।।

मन जो चंचल और अस्थिर है, वह जहाँ-जहाँ भटकता है, वहाँ-वहाँ से उसे नियंत्रित करके आत्मा के वश में लाना चाहिए।

Wherever the restless and unsteady mind wanders, from there it should be restrained and brought back under the control of the self.

Commentary: Krishna offers a practical technique for managing a wandering mind, aligning with modern mindfulness practices.

Shloka 6.29

सर्वं विश्वेन संनादति यद् विश्वेन संनादति।
आत्मन्येव च सर्वं यः पश्यति स सर्वं पश्यति।।

जो योगी सभी में आत्मा को और आत्मा में सभी को देखता है, वह सब कुछ देखता है।

One who sees the self in all beings and all beings in the self sees everything through Yoga.

Commentary: Krishna describes the universal vision of a yogi, fostering compassion and unity, relevant for global harmony.

Shloka 6.30

यो मां पश्यति सर्वत्र सर्वं च मयि पश्यति।
तस्याहं न प्रणश्यामि स च मे न प्रणश्यति।।

जो मुझे सर्वत्र देखता है और सर्वं मुझमें देखता है, उसके लिए मैं नष्ट नहीं होता और वह मेरे लिए नष्ट नहीं होता।

One who sees Me everywhere and sees everything in Me, for them I am never lost, nor are they lost to Me.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes the eternal connection with the divine through universal vision, integrating Bhakti Yoga.

Shloka 6.35

श्री भगवानुवाच
असंशयं महाबाहो मनो दुर्निग्रहं चलं।
अभ्यासेन तु कौन्तेय वैराग्येण च गृह्यते।।

श्री भगवान बोले: हे महाबाहो! निःसंदेह मन चंचल और कठिनता से वश में होने वाला है। परन्तु, हे कुन्तीपुत्र! अभ्यास और वैराग्य से इसे वश में किया जा सकता है।

The Blessed Lord said: Doubtless, O mighty-armed, the mind is restless and hard to control. But, O son of Kunti, it can be controlled through practice and detachment.

Commentary: Krishna reassures Arjuna that the mind can be tamed with persistent practice and detachment, a practical guide for meditation.

Shloka 6.36

असंयतात्मना योगो दुष्प्राप इति मे मतिः।
वश्यात्मना तु यतता शक्योऽवाप्तुमुपायतः।।

मेरी राय में असंयमित आत्मा वाले के लिए योग कठिन है। परन्तु संयमित आत्मा वाला प्रयास करके इसे उपायों से प्राप्त कर सकता है।

Yoga is difficult for one whose mind is uncontrolled, but it is attainable through effort by one whose mind is controlled.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes the importance of effort and discipline in achieving Yoga, encouraging perseverance in spiritual practice.
