Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 16: Daivasura Sampad Vibhaga Yoga

Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 16: Daivasura Sampad Vibhaga Yoga

The Yoga of the Division Between Divine and Demonic Qualities

Chapter Insights

Introduction

Chapter 16 of the Bhagavad Gita, known as Daivasura Sampad Vibhaga Yoga, delineates the qualities that lead to spiritual liberation versus those that result in bondage. Lord Krishna contrasts divine (daivi) qualities, such as fearlessness and purity, with demonic (asuri) qualities, such as arrogance and ignorance. This chapter serves as a moral compass, guiding individuals toward virtuous living and warning against destructive traits. By cultivating divine qualities, one aligns with the path to liberation.

Chapter 16’s practical guidance on character development remains profoundly relevant for fostering ethical behavior in today’s complex world.

Historical and Philosophical Context

Embedded in the Mahabharata, Chapter 16 draws from Vedic and Upanishadic ethics, emphasizing the duality of human nature. Krishna’s discourse (16.1-5) addresses Arjuna’s need for clarity on righteous conduct amidst the Kurukshetra war, integrating Jnana and Karma Yoga to highlight the impact of character on destiny.

Key Themes

  • Divine Qualities: Virtues like fearlessness, purity, and compassion lead to liberation.
  • Demonic Qualities: Traits like arrogance, anger, and ignorance bind one to samsara.
  • Moral Choice: Individuals choose their path through the cultivation of qualities.
  • Consequences of Actions: Demonic traits lead to degradation, while divine traits elevate.
  • Scriptural Guidance: Following scriptures fosters divine qualities and spiritual growth.
  • Self-Awareness: Recognizing one’s tendencies enables transformation toward divinity.

Psychological Insights

The divine qualities (16.1-3) align with positive psychology traits like resilience and empathy, promoting mental well-being. The demonic qualities (16.4-16) reflect destructive patterns like narcissism and impulsivity, which modern psychology links to stress and conflict. Krishna’s call to overcome ignorance (16.24) encourages self-reflection, fostering emotional intelligence.

Cross-Cultural Relevance

The dichotomy of divine and demonic qualities resonates with ethical frameworks in Christianity, Buddhism, and Confucianism, which emphasize virtues over vices. The chapter’s focus on self-discipline (16.22) parallels Stoic and Buddhist practices, making it universally applicable for character development.

Philosophical Debates

Chapter 16 sparks discussions on free will versus destiny, as individuals choose their qualities (16.3-4). The role of scriptures in guiding behavior (16.24) raises questions about authority versus personal ethics, enriching discourse on moral philosophy.

Relevance Today

In 2025, Chapter 16’s teachings guide ethical decision-making in a world facing polarization and materialism. Cultivating divine qualities like compassion and truthfulness fosters unity and integrity, addressing modern challenges like social division and environmental neglect.

Commentators’ Perspectives

Adi Shankaracharya (8th Century)

Shankaracharya views Chapter 16 as a call to transcend demonic qualities through knowledge, seeing divine qualities (16.1-3) as steps toward non-dual realization.

Ramanujacharya (11th Century)

Ramanujacharya interprets Chapter 16 as emphasizing devotion to Krishna, with divine qualities (16.1-3) leading to surrender and liberation in Vishishtadvaita.

Mahatma Gandhi (20th Century)

Gandhi applied Chapter 16’s divine qualities, such as non-violence and truth (16.1-2), to his life, using them to guide his non-violent resistance and service.

Ethical Dilemmas and Resolutions

Arjuna’s Conflict

Arjuna grapples with moral conduct in war. Krishna resolves this by teaching divine qualities like fearlessness and self-control (16.1-3), enabling Arjuna to act righteously without attachment.

Modern Applications

Chapter 16 applies to dilemmas like ethical leadership versus personal gain. Cultivating divine qualities (16.1-3) in professional life fosters integrity and compassion in decision-making.

Meditation and Practice

Jnana Yoga Practice

Reflect on divine qualities (16.1-3) through self-inquiry, identifying and transforming demonic tendencies to align with spiritual growth.

Bhakti Yoga Practice

Meditate on Krishna as the source of divine virtues (16.5), offering actions to him to cultivate purity and devotion.

Cultural Impact

Literature and Arts

Chapter 16 has inspired Indian ethical literature and art, with divine qualities depicted in devotional poetry, music, and temple teachings.

Global Influence

The chapter’s emphasis on virtues has influenced global spiritual movements, including Vedanta and interfaith dialogues, promoting universal ethics and compassion.

Shlokas

Below is a selection of key shlokas from Chapter 16, presented in Sanskrit with Hindi and English translations, accompanied by commentaries to highlight their significance. Chapter 16 has 24 verses, and a representative selection is included.

Shloka 16.1

श्री भगवानुवाच
अभयं सत्त्वसंशुद्धिर्ज्ञानयोगव्यवस्थितिः।
दानं दमश्च यज्ञश्च स्वाध्यायस्तप आर्जवम्।।

श्री भगवान बोले: अभय, सत्त्व की शुद्धि, ज्ञानयोग में स्थिति, दान, दम, यज्ञ, स्वाध्याय, तप और आर्जव।

The Blessed Lord said: Fearlessness, purity of being, steadfastness in knowledge and yoga, charity, self-control, sacrifice, study of scriptures, austerity, and uprightness.

Commentary: Krishna begins listing divine qualities, setting the foundation for virtuous living and liberation.

Shloka 16.2

अहिंसा सत्यमक्रोधस्त्यागः शान्तिरपैशुनम्।
दया भूतेष्वलोलुप्त्वं मार्दवं ह्रीरचापलम्।।

अहिंसा, सत्य, क्रोध का अभाव, त्याग, शांति, अपैशुन, प्राणियों पर दया, लोभ का अभाव, मार्दव, लज्जा और चंचलता का अभाव।

Non-violence, truth, freedom from anger, renunciation, tranquility, absence of slander, compassion for beings, non-covetousness, gentleness, modesty, and steadiness.

Commentary: Krishna continues describing divine qualities, emphasizing compassion and self-discipline as paths to divinity.

Shloka 16.3

तेजः क्षमा धृतिः शौचमद्रोहो नातिमानिता।
भवन्ति सम्पदं दैवीमभिजातस्य भारत।।

तेज, क्षमा, धृति, शौच, अद्रोह और अतिमानिता का अभाव—ये दैवी सम्पदा वाले के लक्षण हैं, हे भारत।

Vigor, forgiveness, fortitude, purity, freedom from malice, and absence of pride—these are the qualities of one born with divine nature, O Bharata.

Commentary: Krishna concludes the divine qualities, highlighting their role in spiritual elevation.

Shloka 16.4

दम्भो दर्पोऽतिमानश्च क्रोधः पारुष्यमेव च।
अज्ञानं चाभिजातस्य पार्थ सम्पदमासुरीम्।।

दम्भ, दर्प, अतिमान, क्रोध, कठोरता और अज्ञान—ये आसुरी सम्पदा वाले के लक्षण हैं, हे पार्थ।

Hypocrisy, arrogance, pride, anger, harshness, and ignorance—these are the qualities of one born with demonic nature, O Partha.

Commentary: Krishna introduces demonic qualities, warning of their role in spiritual bondage.

Shloka 16.5

दैवी सम्पद्विमोक्षाय निबन्धायासुरी मता।
मा शुचः सम्पदं दैवीमभिजातोऽसि पाण्डव।।

दैवी सम्पदा मोक्ष के लिए और आसुरी सम्पदा बंधन के लिए मानी जाती है। शोक मत कर, हे पाण्डव, तू दैवी सम्पदा में जन्मा है।

Divine qualities lead to liberation, while demonic qualities lead to bondage. Do not grieve, O Pandava, for you are born with divine nature.

Commentary: Krishna reassures Arjuna of his divine nature, encouraging cultivation of virtuous traits.

Shloka 16.6

द्वौ भूतसर्गौ लोकेऽस्मिन्दैव आसुर एव च।
दैवो विस्तरशः प्रोक्त आसुरं पार्थ मे शृणु।।

इस लोक में दो प्रकार के भूत सर्ग हैं—दैव और आसुर। दैव का विस्तार से कहा गया, अब आसुर के बारे में मुझसे सुन, हे पार्थ।

There are two types of beings in this world: divine and demonic. The divine has been described; now hear about the demonic, O Partha.

Commentary: Krishna sets the stage for describing demonic tendencies, contrasting them with divine virtues.

Shloka 16.7

प्रवृत्तिं च निवृत्तिं च जना न विदुरासुराः।
न शौचं नापि चाचारो न सत्यं तेषु विद्यते।।

आसुर लोग न प्रवृत्ति जानते हैं, न निवृत्ति। उनमें न शौच, न आचार, न सत्य होता है।

Demonic beings know neither action nor renunciation; neither purity, nor conduct, nor truth is found in them.

Commentary: Krishna highlights the ignorance of demonic beings, lacking moral and spiritual grounding.

Shloka 16.8

असत्यमप्रतिष्ठं ते जगदाहुरनीश्वरम्।
अपरस्परसम्भूतं किमन्यत्कामहैतुकम्।।

वे कहते हैं कि जगत असत्य, अप्रतिष्ठ और ईश्वररहित है, केवल परस्पर संयोग से उत्पन्न, और क्या? केवल कामना से प्रेरित।

They say the world is unreal, without foundation, godless, born of mutual union, caused by desire alone—what else?

Commentary: Krishna describes the nihilistic worldview of demonic beings, rejecting divine order.

Shloka 16.10

काममाश्रित्य दुष्पूरं दम्भमानमदान्विताः।
मोहाद्गृहीत्वासद्ग्राहान्प्रवर्तन्तेऽशुचिव्रताः।।

दुष्पूर कामनाओं का आश्रय लेकर, दम्भ, मान और मद से युक्त, मोह से असत् को ग्रहण करके, अशुद्ध व्रतों में प्रवृत्त होते हैं।

Clinging to insatiable desires, full of hypocrisy, pride, and arrogance, holding false views through delusion, they act with impure resolves.

Commentary: Krishna warns of the destructive cycle of desire and delusion in demonic behavior.

Shloka 16.11

चिन्तामपरिमेयां च प्रलयान्तामुपाश्रिताः।
कामोपभोगपरमा एतावदिति निश्चिताः।।

अपरिमित चिंता और मृत्यु तक की चिंता में लीन, कामभोग को परम मानने वाले, यही सब कुछ है—ऐसा निश्चय करने वाले।

Obsessed with endless anxieties till death, considering sense enjoyment the highest aim, convinced that this is all.

Commentary: Krishna highlights the futility and anxiety of a materialistic mindset.

Shloka 16.12

आशापाशशतैर्बद्धाः कामक्रोधपरायणाः।
ईहन्ते कामभोगार्थमच्यन्तमधनं प्रति।।

आशा के सैकड़ों पाशों से बंधे, काम और क्रोध में तत्पर, कामभोग के लिए अनुचित धन की इच्छा करते हैं।

Bound by hundreds of ties of desire, given to lust and anger, they strive to amass wealth by unjust means for sense gratification.

Commentary: Krishna describes the bondage of demonic beings to desire, leading to unethical actions.

Shloka 16.16

अनेकचित्तविभ्रान्ता मोहजालसमावृताः।
प्रसक्ताः कामभोगेषु पतन्ति नरकेऽशुचौ।।

अनेक चित्तों से भ्रमित, मोहजाल में फँसे, कामभोगों में आसक्त, वे अशुद्ध नरक में गिरते हैं।

Bewildered by many thoughts, entangled in the web of delusion, addicted to sense pleasures, they fall into a foul hell.

Commentary: Krishna warns of the consequences of demonic traits, leading to spiritual degradation.

Shloka 16.21

त्रिविधं नरकस्येदं द्वारं नाशनमात्मनः।
कामः क्रोधस्तथा लोभस्तस्मादेतत्त्रयं त्यजेत्।।

काम, क्रोध और लोभ—ये आत्मा के नाश के तीन नरक के द्वार हैं। इसलिए इन तीनों को त्याग देना चाहिए।

Lust, anger, and greed—these are the three gates to hell, leading to the soul’s destruction. Thus, one must abandon these three.

Commentary: Krishna identifies the root causes of demonic behavior, urging their abandonment for spiritual progress.

Shloka 16.22

एतैर्विमुक्तः कौन्तेय तमोद्वारैस्त्रिभिर्नरः।
आचरत्यात्मनः श्रेयस्ततो याति परां गतिम्।।

हे कौन्तेय! इन तीन तमोद्वारों से मुक्त व्यक्ति आत्मा का कल्याण करता है और फिर परम गति को प्राप्त करता है।

One who is freed from these three gates of darkness, O son of Kunti, acts for the soul’s welfare and attains the supreme goal.

Commentary: Krishna emphasizes liberation through overcoming destructive traits, leading to spiritual fulfillment.

Shloka 16.24

तस्माच्छास्त्रं प्रमाणं ते कार्याकार्यव्यवस्थितौ।
ज्ञात्वा शास्त्रविधानोक्तं कर्म कर्तुमिहार्हसि।।

इसलिए कार्य और अकार्य की व्यवस्था में शास्त्र ही प्रमाण है। शास्त्र के विधान को जानकर तुझे यहाँ कर्म करना चाहिए।

Therefore, let the scriptures be your authority in determining what should be done or not done. Knowing the scriptural injunctions, you should act accordingly.

Commentary: Krishna concludes by emphasizing the role of scriptures in guiding ethical and spiritual actions.
